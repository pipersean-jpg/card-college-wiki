    Card Switches¶

    Sources: CC4 Ch. 40 (pp. 809–834)

    What Card Switches Are¶

    A card switch secretly exchanges one card for a different card. Where a color change is a visible, theatrical transformation, a card switch is an invisible exchange — the audience never sees the switch happen. They are simply holding, looking at, or finding a card that is different from what they originally had.

    Switches are used to: - Correct a force failure (the wrong card was taken) - Load the correct card into an "impossible" location - Exchange a chosen card for a gimmicked version (a signed card, a marked card, a special card) - Clean up after a complex multi-phase effect

    The Curry Turnover Change¶

    Attributed to: Stewart James (or Paul Curry — sources vary; Giobbi attributes it in context)

    A card lying face down on the table is turned face up by using another card to flip it — but in the turning action, the card on the table is secretly switched for the one doing the flipping. The audience sees a card turned face up, but the card now face up is different from the card that was on the table.

    The cover is the natural turning action itself — the two cards overlap briefly, and the switch happens during the overlap.

    The Simple Turnover Switch¶

    A simplified version of the turnover switch using a slightly different angle and pressure.

    The Card-spread Switch¶

    A card is switched while it is being spread among others — the switch happens during the spreading action, which provides visual complexity that hides the exchange.

    The Allerton Change¶

    Attributed to: Allerton

    A switch performed while the cards are in a spread position in both hands.

    The Kaps Switch¶

    Attributed to: Fred Kaps

    Fred Kaps was a Dutch magician known for elegant close-up work. This switch is named for him and involves a specific handling during which one card replaces another while the deck is momentarily in an unusual position.

    The "Hofzinser" Top Change¶

    Attributed to: Johann Nepomuk Hofzinser

    A variant of the top change with a different hand position — the card is held differently than in the standard Robert-Houdin version, giving a different angle of concealment. Giobbi uses quotation marks around "Hofzinser" to signal some uncertainty about whether Hofzinser was the actual originator or whether the attribution was added later.

    This reflects a general principle Giobbi notes in his introduction: attributions in magic history are frequently uncertain, passed down through multiple retellings, and sometimes attributed to famous names for prestige rather than accuracy.

    The Push-in Change¶

    A card is pushed face-down into the face-up deck — and when it is pushed in, it has changed. The "push-in" motion creates a brief window where the switch occurs.

    The Glide Change¶

    Related to The Glide. The card that appears to be dealt from the face of a face-down deck is actually the second card — the glide retains the face card while the second card is dealt. By setting up differently, a specific card can be switched in during the dealing action.

    The Mexican Turnover¶

    Attributed to: Alex Elmsley

    Despite the name, this move has nothing culturally Mexican — the name refers to the visual action. A card lying face down on the table is picked up using another face-down card as a lever. During the pick-up action, the two cards exchange positions: what was on the table ends up in the hand, and what was in the hand ends up on the table — but the audience cannot tell which is which.

    This is one of the most useful switches for table workers: it appears completely open (nothing is hidden, the actions are above the table), yet two cards exchange invisibly.

    Attribution Debate: The Turnover Switches¶

    The "Curry Turnover Change" name signals Paul Curry as one possible originator; other sources attribute variations of this family of moves to different inventors. The general class of "card turned over by another card" switches appears in magic literature going back well before any specific modern attribution. Giobbi is careful to note attributions as he understands them while acknowledging the historical uncertainty.
