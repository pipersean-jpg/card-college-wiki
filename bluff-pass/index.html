    The Bluff Pass¶

    Sources: CC3 Ch. 30 (pp. 555–558)

    What This Is¶

    The Bluff Pass is a method for controlling a selected card to the top of the deck that looks indistinguishable from a series of genuine cuts. Despite its name — "pass" — it is technically a double-cut sequence, not a true pass (which invisibly transposes two halves of the deck). The name refers to the intent of the move: it bluffs the audience into believing a fair cut has been made.

    Why It's Called a Pass¶

    The term "pass" is sometimes used loosely in magic to mean any control that moves a card from one position to another. Giobbi is careful to distinguish: the Classic Pass (CC2 Ch. 19) transposes the two halves of the deck in a single action. The Bluff Pass achieves a similar result by sequencing two or more legitimate cuts — no single action is secretly deceptive; the deception lies in the combination.

    How It Works¶

    With a little-finger break beneath the selected card (which has been returned to the center):

    1. The right hand cuts the packet above the break and places it below the left hand's packet
    2. Immediately, the right hand cuts approximately half the deck from the top and places it on the bottom again

    The two-cut sequence neutralizes each other: the first cut brings the selected card one move toward the top; the second brings everything back in the right relationship. The net result is the selected card on top, yet the audience has seen two fair-looking cuts.

    Executed at a natural pace with relaxed hands, the two cuts read as a complete and thorough mixing of the deck.

    The Critical Insight¶

    The deception does not require technical speed or cover. It works because:

    1. Spectators do not track the position of individual cards through multiple cuts
    2. Two cuts feel "thorough" — more so than one
    3. The move is done in the open, not hidden — there is nothing to hide

    This makes the Bluff Pass much more accessible to performers who have not yet mastered the Classic Pass, while achieving a highly similar result in most performance conditions.

    Limitations¶

    The Bluff Pass does not work as a "magic moment" — it cannot be used to explain a sudden appearance or disappearance in the way a genuine pass can. It is purely a control: the card ends up in a known position, but there is no single invisible moment around which a revelation can be built. For that, the Classic Pass is superior.
