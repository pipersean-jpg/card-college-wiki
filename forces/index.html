    Forces — Overview¶

    Sources: CC1 Ch. 5 (pp. 83–92); CC1 Ch. 15 (pp. 215–228); CC4 Ch. 39 (pp. 791–804)

    What a Force Is¶

    A force is a technique that leads a spectator to "freely" choose a card predetermined by the performer, while the spectator believes their selection was entirely their own choice.

    The difference between a force and a control: with a control, the spectator genuinely selects any card and the performer manages its location afterward. With a force, the performer determines which card is selected in the first place.

    Forces are among the most powerful tools in card magic because they eliminate the need to "find" a card — the performer already knows what the spectator has before the trick begins.

    Why Forces Work¶

    Forces exploit several psychological principles:

    Free will illusion: Spectators believe they have complete freedom of choice. In most force situations, this belief is genuine — the spectator has not been forced to do something they could not resist. The illusion of free choice is not coercive; it's a matter of design.

    Limited tracking: During fast or fluid actions (a riffle, a spread, a dribble), spectators cannot track individual card positions. The performer exploits specific moments when the eye cannot follow what the hand is doing.

    Psychological moment: The classic force exploits the observation that in a spreading action, spectators tend to remove a card from the center-right area — and they do this with predictable consistency.

    The Three Categories of Forces¶

    Mechanical Forces¶

    The force depends on a specific physical maneuver that gives the spectator only one option, even though it appears they have many. The Riffle Force is the clearest example: the card the spectator stops at has been positioned there.

    Psychological Forces¶

    The force works by understanding what spectators tend to do naturally. The Classic Force is the primary example: the deck is spread, and the spectator will most often take from a specific region.

    Mathematical Forces¶

    The force works through counting or mathematical structure. The Ten-to-twenty force (CC1 Ch. 5) uses arithmetic to ensure a specific card is reached regardless of where the spectator begins counting.

    Survey of Forces by Volume¶

    Volume 1, Chapter 5 — First Forces¶

    The Crisscross Force (a.k.a. the Balducci Force / Cross-cut Force)
    A spectator cuts the deck and the top section is turned crosswise. After a moment of discussion, the performer points to the card "at the cut" — which is actually the original top card of the deck. This force relies entirely on psychology: the brief delay causes the spectator to forget the original position of the two halves. Giobbi attributes this to Ed Balducci. It is widely known; savvy audiences may have seen it.

    The Goldin Force
    A force using a specific spreading action attributed to Goldin. The performer spreads cards and positions the force card for the spectator to take.

    The Ten-to-twenty Force
    A mathematical force: the spectator thinks of a number between 10 and 20, counts down that many cards, adds the digits of their number, and counts down that many more — arriving at the force card.

    Outs (CC1 Ch. 15)¶

    A crucial concept: no force succeeds 100% of the time. An out is an alternative procedure the performer uses when a force fails — a way to pivot so the trick still works even though the spectator took the wrong card.

    Giobbi discusses outs explicitly in CC1 Ch. 15: - Direct outs: The performer immediately adapts the trick to whatever card was taken - Indirect outs: The performer uses a brief moment of misdirection to switch the taken card for the force card

    Performers who know their outs approach the Classic Force with confidence rather than anxiety — and that confidence itself helps the force succeed.

    Force vs. Control: The Philosophy¶

    Giobbi reflects throughout the series that forces are generally superior to controls when the effect allows. A force means: - No need to locate the card - No risk of the card being lost - Cleaner structure: the performer chooses what happens before the trick begins

    The downside: forces require setup (positioning the card in advance) and the force method must match the performance context. A force visible to one spectator cannot be used for a group.
