# Estimation

**Sources:** CC4 Ch. 43 (pp. 877–893)

---

## What Estimation Is

**Estimation** is the skill of accurately judging how many cards are in a packet — either by looking at it (visual estimation) or by feeling it (tactile estimation). It sounds mundane, but in the context of card magic, the ability to cut accurately to a specific number of cards from the top is essential for many gambling demonstrations and mathematical effects.

---

## Visual Estimation (CC4 Ch. 43)

The performer practices estimating packet sizes by eye. A deck of 52 cards has a known thickness; 26 cards is half that; 13 cards is a quarter. With practice, the performer learns to judge these proportions accurately enough to cut to within one or two cards of a target number.

### An Application

The performer asks a spectator to cut any number of cards from the deck. The performer estimates the cut size, divides the remainder visually, and appears to know exactly how many cards were cut — achieved through estimation rather than counting.

---

## Location Using Outs

A practical approach when exact estimation is impossible: the performer estimates and then uses an **out** to adjust. If the estimate is close but not exact, a slight adjustment (dealing off one or two extra cards, or taking one back) can correct the count within the performance context without the audience realizing.

Giobbi frames outs in estimation as part of the broader principle of out management: no technique is perfect 100% of the time, so preparing adjustments that look like continuations of the performance is essential.

---

## Fingernail Estimation

A tactile method: the performer runs a thumbnail along the edge of the deck, and the number of ridges (the edges of individual cards) can be counted by feel. This is used when the performer needs to cut to an exact number and must count the cards without looking.

Fingernail estimation is more accurate than visual estimation but more detectable: the thumbing action is visible. It is best used when the performer's other hand is providing attention-management (pointing, talking, adjusting something), so the thumbing hand is not watched.

---

## Estimation in Context

The main contexts for estimation in Giobbi's series:

- **Gambling demonstrations:** The performer cuts to the exact number of cards needed to deal a winning hand to a specific seat
- **Mental effects:** Apparently knowing how many cards a spectator cut without counting
- **Setting up positions:** After a spectator cuts, estimating where a setup card has moved to

Giobbi notes that visual estimation is a passive skill that improves simply through handling cards frequently — performers who handle cards daily for years develop accurate intuitions without deliberate practice. The more deliberate skill of fingernail estimation must be actively practiced.
