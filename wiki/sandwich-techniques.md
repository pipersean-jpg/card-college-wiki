# Sandwich Techniques

**Sources:** CC4 Ch. 47 (pp. 947–957)

---

## What Sandwich Techniques Are

In card magic, a **sandwich** is a presentation in which a selected card appears between two face-up indicator cards — typically two Jokers, two face cards, or two identically suited cards. The two face-up cards "find" or "trap" the selection by sandwiching it.

Sandwich techniques are the sleights used to create this effect: loading a card between two face-up cards, displaying the sandwich, and switching or revealing the card within the sandwich.

---

## The Display Load

The fundamental sandwich load. Two face-up indicator cards are held in the right hand spread slightly apart. The selection (held in the left hand, face down) is apparently shown to be separate from the indicators. In the display of the three cards, the selection is secretly slipped between the two face-up cards without being seen.

The "display" provides cover: the audience is watching the faces of the indicator cards and the relationship between the three cards. The loading action happens in the moments of looking-at-faces.

---

## The Direct Load

A more streamlined load where the selection is loaded between the indicators in a single, direct motion rather than through a display sequence. Faster and cleaner, but requires more precise timing.

---

## The Kosky Switch

**Named for:** Kosky

A switching technique used within the sandwich context: the card that is apparently the selection inside the sandwich is secretly exchanged for the real selection during a display or handling action.

This solves a practical problem: sometimes the performer cannot load the selection directly into the sandwich during the loading sequence. The Kosky Switch allows the performer to load a neutral card first and then switch in the selection.

---

## The Simple Sandwich Load

A basic load used in less complex situations — when the performer has more freedom of handling (fewer angles, more time, fewer spectators).

---

## Structural Role in Performance

Giobbi notes that sandwich effects have a specific dramaturgical structure: the two indicator cards are established as the "detective" cards; the selection is established as the "fugitive" or "prisoner"; the sandwich revelation is the capture. This narrative structure is what makes sandwich tricks feel complete and satisfying.

The techniques in CC4 Ch. 47 are the mechanical tools that make this narrative work. Giobbi emphasizes that the technical loading should be invisible but that the theatrical framing is what the audience experiences.
