# Lapping

**Sources:** CC4 Ch. 48 (pp. 959–967)

---

## What Lapping Is

**Lapping** is the use of the lap — the space between the performer's thighs when seated at a table — as a hidden holding area. A card (or small packet) is secretly dropped into the lap, where it can be held out of play until needed, then picked up later.

The lap is one of the most practical secret locations in close-up magic because it is completely out of sight when the performer is seated, it requires no technical sleight to use, and a card placed there is genuinely hidden (not palmed in a hand, where it could potentially be seen).

---

## General Observations on Lapping (CC4 Ch. 48)

Giobbi opens with the context: lapping is only possible when the performer is **seated at a table**. It is irrelevant to standing performers. For close-up workers who primarily sit (as at a card table or intimate setting), it is one of the most powerful tools available.

He also notes the psychological advantage: because the lap is completely out of view, there is no angle problem. A palmed card in the right hand must be positioned correctly for every spectator; a lapped card is simply invisible to everyone.

---

## The Bottom-card Lap

A card is lapped from the bottom of the deck. The deck is casually placed on the table — or positioned near the table's edge — and the bottom card is allowed to slide into the lap as the deck lands. The bottom card is never seen by spectators.

This is the simplest and most direct lapping method. It requires no additional handling and can be executed casually during conversation.

---

## The All-around Square-up Lap

During the all-around square-up action (squaring the deck from all four sides), the deck is momentarily brought near the table's edge. One card is released into the lap during the squaring. The all-around action provides complete cover — spectators see normal squaring, not a lap drop.

---

## Jean-Claude Pages's Lapping Method

**Attributed to:** Jean-Claude Pages

A specific lapping method by French close-up worker Jean-Claude Pages. His technique uses a slight body adjustment as cover — a natural forward lean that brings the deck closer to the lap without looking deliberate.

---

## The Lap Control

A complete sequence: a card is lapped, the deck is handled normally for a phase or two, and then the lapped card is secretly retrieved from the lap and added back to the deck. The card appears to have "traveled" from the deck, done something impossible, and returned.

Giobbi discusses the psychological importance of timing: the card must be retrieved from the lap at a moment when the hands naturally pass through the lap area — reaching into a pocket, adjusting a jacket, or any motion that brings the hand near the lap.

---

## The Trousers-fold Holdout

A more elaborate version where a card is held in a fold of the trouser fabric — a small crease that holds the card against the inner thigh. This is a temporary holdout rather than a true lap (the card is not free in the lap but secured against the fabric) and is used when the performer must stand briefly while still needing to hold out a card.

---

## The Knee Holdout

A card is pressed between the knees while seated. More secure than lapping but less clean to execute and retrieve. Used in specific situations where a lap drop would be difficult.
