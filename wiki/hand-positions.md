# Hand Positions and Basic Grips

**Sources:** CC1 Ch. 1 (pp. 13–36)

---

## What This Is

Before any sleight can be learned, a card worker needs a consistent, natural way to hold the deck. Giobbi opens *Card College* with three fundamental grip positions because every technique in the five-volume course builds on them. These are not sleights — they are the neutral, resting positions from which sleights begin and to which the hands return.

---

## The Three Basic Grips

### Dealing Position (Left Hand)

The most important grip in card magic. The deck lies face down across the left hand, held by four main pressure points:

- **Left thumb** rests on top, pointing toward the outer right corner
- **Left fingers** curl under the deck; the middle, ring, and little fingers press against the right long edge
- **Left index finger** wraps around the outer short end of the deck and presses lightly upward on the face
- The deck rests in the fork of the thumb, in the flesh between thumb and index finger

The deck should be comfortable and under complete control. The left thumb does most of the work — it can push cards right (for a spread), and it can press down to maintain even pressure across the top. From dealing position, nearly every left-hand sleight originates.

### End Grip (Right Hand)

The right hand holds the deck at its short ends (the "ends" in card magic terminology, as opposed to the long sides which are called "sides" or "edges"):

- **Thumb** contacts the inner short end (the end closest to the performer)
- **Middle, ring, and little fingers** contact the outer short end
- **Index finger** curls over and rests lightly on top of the deck

This grip is used whenever the deck passes to the right hand for a shuffle, cut, or display. It gives maximum control at the ends for riffling and for producing breaks.

### Elevated Dealing Position

A variation of dealing position where the deck is held higher in the hand — toward the fingertips rather than deep in the palm. This becomes relevant for certain palming techniques where a deeper palm grip would expose the back of the hand uncomfortably.

---

## Why These Positions Matter

Giobbi is insistent on precision here because **consistency creates invisibility**. A spectator who sees the deck held the same way at all times has no visual trigger to notice when something changes. The moment a performer tightens their grip awkwardly or shifts the deck into an unusual position, attention focuses on the hands.

The principle: the rest position should look identical to the action position.

---

## The Swing Cut

Also taught in Chapter 1, the swing cut is the natural, one-handed cut most people do automatically. The top half of the deck pivots outward on the left thumb, swings to the left, and is placed below the bottom half. It appears casual because it *is* casual — and that quality makes it the preferred method for cutting under normal circumstances.
