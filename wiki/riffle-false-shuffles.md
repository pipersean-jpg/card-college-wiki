# Riffle False Shuffles

**Sources:** CC3 Ch. 33 (pp. 627–660)

---

## Overview

Riffle (dovetail) shuffles are the standard in most Western card culture — they're what card players do. A false riffle shuffle that looks genuinely random is therefore one of the most valuable skills for a card worker, because it passes scrutiny even from audiences who are card-savvy.

Giobbi covers riffle false shuffles comprehensively in CC3 Ch. 33, building on the basic open and closed riffle shuffle taught in CC1 Ch. 7.

---

## The Basic Riffle Shuffles (CC1 Ch. 7)

Before studying false shuffles, Giobbi establishes two styles:

**The Closed Riffle Shuffle:** Both halves are riffled together face down, then the interlaced corners are pushed together and the deck is squared. This is the standard American shuffle.

**The Open Riffle Shuffle:** The two halves are held with their faces visible during the shuffle and then cascaded together. This is more elegant visually and more common in European close-up performance.

**The Waterfall Riffle Shuffle:** A flourishing version where the interlaced corners are bowed downward, creating a cascading waterfall effect. Used primarily as a visual display; it does not lend itself to stock controls.

**Riffle Shuffle Stock Controls (CC1 Ch. 7):** By controlling how deeply the packets interleave at the top and bottom, the performer can preserve a stock at the top or bottom while genuinely mixing the middle. The left thumb releases the final few cards of its packet last (keeping a top stock on top), or the first few cards first (keeping a bottom stock on the bottom).

---

## The Pull-Through False Shuffle (CC3 Ch. 33)

The most versatile riffle false shuffle for the standing performer (in-the-hands). Both halves are riffled together genuinely, creating an interlocked mesh. Then, instead of squaring the two interlocked halves together, one packet is **pulled through** the other — the two halves slide past each other back to their original separated state, then squared.

To the audience, this looks like a normal riffle shuffle completed with a final squaring action. The deck is returned to *exactly* its pre-shuffle order.

The cover for the pull-through is the natural squaring motion that concludes any riffle shuffle. The hands must come together smoothly, and the pull-through must happen during the squaring action with no hesitation.

---

## Vernon's Pull-Through Shuffle Variant (CC3 Ch. 33)

**Attributed to:** Dai Vernon

A handling refinement on the pull-through that makes the action slightly different — Vernon's hands position the packets differently before the pull, making the move easier to execute cleanly from certain angles. Giobbi presents both versions and recommends the performer choose based on which feel is more natural.

---

## The Triumph Shuffle (CC3 Ch. 33)

A specialized shuffle sequence designed to set up the **Triumph effect** — where half the deck is face-up and half face-down, and a magical gesture or shake restores all cards face-down except the selected one.

The Triumph shuffle is not a false shuffle in the conventional sense: it *does* mix the deck, but in a controlled way. The face-up and face-down halves are interleaved, then the performer later appears to straighten them all out magically. Giobbi credits the basic Triumph plot to **Dai Vernon** and teaches a complete routine around it (the trick "Triumph" appears in CC3).

---

## Controlling the Top Stock with a Riffle Shuffle (CC3 Ch. 33)

A sequence that uses the riffle shuffle's mechanical properties to preserve the entire top stock (any number of cards) while genuinely mixing the bottom portion. This is more deceptive than the basic stock control from CC1 because it involves a complete riffle interleave — the audience sees what looks like a full riffle shuffle.

---

## A Combination False Shuffle (CC3 Ch. 33)

A compound false shuffle combining overhand and riffle elements — the deck appears to be shuffled twice using different techniques, while the order is preserved throughout. The combination is particularly strong because it addresses the skeptical spectator who might query one shuffle type.

---

## The In-the-Hands False Shuffle (CC3 Ch. 33)

A complete false riffle shuffle performed with both hands in the air (not on the table). This is more visual than the tabled Zarrow shuffle (see [The Zarrow Shuffle](zarrow-shuffle.md)) and more appropriate for standing performers.

---

## The Block Transfer (CC3 Ch. 33)

A technique for moving a complete block of cards from one position in the deck to another, disguised as part of a riffle shuffle. Used when a set-up is needed in the middle of a deck rather than the top or bottom.

---

## What the Sources Agree On

Riffle false shuffles are unequivocally more convincing for card-savvy audiences than overhand false shuffles. The trade-off is difficulty: the pull-through in particular requires hours of practice to execute without the packets catching on each other or producing an audible scraping sound.

Giobbi notes that many professional performers use a **combination approach**: riffle shuffle with stock control for the bottom half, pull-through for the top half, giving the appearance of thorough mixing while preserving an important setup.
