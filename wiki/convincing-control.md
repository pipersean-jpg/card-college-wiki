# The Convincing Control

**Sources:** CC3 Ch. 30 (pp. 551–558)

**Originator:** Dai Vernon

---

## What This Is

The Convincing Control is a method for controlling a selected card to the top of the deck while making it appear as though the deck was cleanly cut with a completely fair result. It is one of the most widely-used intermediate controls in card magic and is considered a masterpiece of Vernon's approach: the technique exploits the psychology of what spectators *expect* to see rather than relying on speed or concealment.

---

## How It Works

1. The spectator's card has been returned and a break is held beneath it (the card is on top, with a little-finger break under it)
2. The performer cuts the deck: the right hand takes the top portion above the break and places it below as though making a complete, fair cut
3. The left hand's packet (the selected card is now on top) is placed on top of the right hand's packet
4. The result looks like a clean, full cut of the deck — but the selected card is now on top

The deception hinges on a crucial psychology: spectators see a "cut" and interpret it as a mixing action. They do not track the relative position of individual card groups. The Convincing Control exploits this habitual misperception.

---

## The Handling Refinement

Giobbi notes that the action should be fluid and *unhurried* — one of Vernon's core insights. A fast Convincing Control looks like a quick maneuver; a slow, deliberate one looks like a genuine cut. The performer should take their time, making the motion feel considered rather than rushed.

The position of the break at the moment of cutting is crucial: the break must be at the natural cutting depth (roughly half the deck), not obviously close to the top or bottom, or the cut will look suspicious.

---

## Refinements and Variations

Giobbi discusses several handling refinements in CC3 Ch. 30 that make the move more deceptive in different contexts:

- **Spreading the deck** before the cut (so the spectator sees no card near the top)
- **Allowing a spectator to cut** at the break — the selected card ends in the middle of the deck, yet the performer knows its location
- **Using the convincing control as a palm get-ready** — the break positions the top card for an immediate top palm after the cut

---

## The Convincing Control Palm

Giobbi teaches this variation in CC3 Ch. 38 in connection with the side steal. As the Convincing Control cut is made, the top card is simultaneously palmed by the right hand. The audience sees what appears to be a clean cut; the performer has secretly removed the selected card from the deck entirely.

---

## What the Sources Say

Giobbi attributes the Convincing Control to **Dai Vernon**. It appears across multiple magic books and is closely associated with Vernon's influence on the second half of the 20th century. The move exemplifies Vernon's philosophy: "be natural" and "use what the spectator already believes."

There is no significant debate about attribution — the method is universally credited to Vernon. The refinements and specific handlings vary by teacher.
