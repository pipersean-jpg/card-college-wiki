# Palming — Overview

**Sources:** CC2 Ch. 18 (pp. 269–295); CC3 Ch. 36 (pp. 703–730)

---

## What Palming Is

**Palming** is secretly holding one or more cards in the hand while the hand appears empty or natural. The palmed cards are invisible to spectators because they are hidden by the hand's normal posture.

Palming is used to:
- Remove a card from the deck without anyone seeing
- Add a card to the deck without anyone seeing
- Hold a card during another action (producing it, switching it, loading it elsewhere)
- Secretly transport a card from the deck to a pocket, another location, or another prop

---

## Why Palming Is Difficult

The difficulty of palming is not technical — the action itself is simple. The difficulty is **naturalness**: a hand holding a palmed card will feel different to the performer, and that self-consciousness often manifests as stiffness or awkwardness visible to spectators.

Giobbi identifies the central challenge early in CC2 Ch. 18: the performer must believe — and make the audience believe — that the palming hand is completely empty. The technique achieves the mechanics; the performance achieves the belief.

---

## The Palm Grip (CC2 Ch. 18)

All palms begin with the card in the **palm grip**: the card lies diagonally across the palm of the right hand, held in place by slight muscular tension and the natural concavity of the palm. The card is held by:

- Pressure from the heel of the thumb on the inner left corner
- Pressure from the curved ring and little finger at the outer right corner
- The palm's natural concavity maintaining the card's alignment

The fingers curl slightly — naturally, not dramatically — and the hand can gesture and move without exposing the card.

---

## Concealing the Palmed Card (CC2 Ch. 18)

The palmed card is only invisible if the hand is angled correctly. Giobbi lists the most important principles:

1. **Never angle the palm toward the audience** — keep the back of the hand toward the audience, not the face
2. **Move the hand as little as possible** while it is loaded — a hand that gestures naturally attracts less attention than one that is artificially still
3. **The eyes lead** — if the performer's gaze is forward or toward the spectator, the spectator's gaze follows. Looking at the palming hand telegraphs it
4. **Matching the opposite hand** — the palming hand should move in rhythm and shape with the non-palming hand. Asymmetric hands look suspicious

---

## Theoretical Considerations (CC2 Ch. 18)

Giobbi includes a theoretical section before any technique, arguing that understanding *why* palming works prepares the performer to execute it convincingly.

Key point: spectators do not watch both hands simultaneously. They follow the hand that appears to be doing something. The palming hand should appear to be doing nothing (or the less interesting action), which directs attention away from it.

He also notes that palming is easier at a table than standing: a natural hand position at the table keeps the back of the hand toward spectators automatically.

### Additional Palms Covered in CC3 Ch. 36

**The One-handed Palm:** Palming a card from the top of the deck using only the right hand, without any left hand action. Used when the left hand is occupied or when the performer wants to palm during an action where the left hand cannot help.

**The Transfer Palm:** A card is palmed from the left hand into the right (or vice versa) during an apparently casual transfer of the deck.

**The Loewy Palm:** A specific palm position attributed to **Loewy**, offering a different angle of concealment than the standard palm grip.

**The Left-hand Bottom Palm:** Palming a card from the bottom of the deck into the left hand. Technically more demanding than the right-hand top palm.

**The Right-hand Bottom Palm:** Palming from the bottom into the right hand.

## Replacing Palmed Cards (CC2 Ch. 18)

Just as important as removing a card is replacing it without detection. Giobbi teaches multiple replacing techniques:

- **On top:** During a squaring action, the right hand (holding the palmed card) meets the left hand's deck — the palmed card rides back onto the top naturally
- **On the bottom:** The palm is inverted and the card is added to the bottom during a cut or shuffle
- **In the middle:** Less common, but possible through specific mechanics

---

## The Gambler's Cop (CC3 Ch. 36)

A palm grip where the card is held not in the center of the palm but **at the inner end** — tucked against the heel of the hand. The cop provides better angles than the standard palm in some situations. See [Bottom Palm and Gambler's Cop](bottom-palm.md).

---

## Norman Houghton's Pocket Extraction (CC3 Ch. 36)

A sequence for producing a palmed card from the jacket pocket — reaching into the pocket, leaving the palmed card, then "finding" it there. The technique makes it appear the card has traveled to the pocket by magic.
