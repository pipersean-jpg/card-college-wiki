# Controls — Overview

**Sources:** CC1 Ch. 4 (pp. 65–82); CC3 Ch. 30 (pp. 549–568)

---

## What a Control Is

A **control** is any technique that secretly moves a chosen card from where a spectator replaced it to a position the performer knows — almost always the top or bottom of the deck.

When a spectator selects a card, approves it, and returns it to the deck, they believe the card is lost among 51 others. The performer's job is to know exactly where it is. From that known position, the card can be revealed, palmed, switched, or forced.

Controls are among the most important sleights in all of card magic. Giobbi returns to the subject across three separate chapters (CC1 Ch. 4, CC2 Chs. 17/19, CC3 Ch. 30), each introducing more sophisticated approaches.

---

## The Two Structural Approaches

### Direct Controls

The card is moved to the top or bottom during a single clean action — often the shuffle the performer makes immediately after the card is returned. Spectators see what appears to be an honest shuffle or cut.

### Indirect Controls (The Return Itself)

The card is controlled *as* it is replaced — the spectator inserts their card into what appears to be the middle but is actually positioned to end up on top or bottom. The overhand shuffle control is the clearest example: the card is injogged as it is returned, and the shuffle that follows brings it to the top.

## Survey of Controls in the Giobbi Series

### Volume 1 (Chapter 4)

**The Overhand Shuffle Control**  
The performer riffles the deck, the spectator inserts the card. The performer breaks the shuffle above the inserted card, jogging it, then shuffles off and runs it to the top. Clean and natural-looking. See [Overhand Shuffle Controls](overhand-shuffle-controls.md).

**The "Whoops!" Control**  
The card is replaced in a spread and then "accidentally" becomes the top card when the deck closes. The "accident" provides the misdirection.

**The Diagonal Insertion**  
The spectator's card is pushed diagonally into the spread so that its corner jogs out. The performer squares the deck with a specific action that secretly elevates the card to the top. See [Overhand Shuffle Controls](overhand-shuffle-controls.md).

**The Peek Control**  
After peeking at (secretly learning the identity of) a card by having the spectator riffle and stop, the performer controls that card. Multiple variants — including one for two cards and one for multiple cards.

**The One-card Middle Pass**  
Moving a single card from the middle directly to the top. This is a simplified form of the classic pass and is used when only one card needs moving.

### Volume 3 (Chapter 30)

**The Convincing Control**  
Dai Vernon's masterpiece for controlling a selected card while making the entire handling look completely fair. See [The Convincing Control](convincing-control.md).

**The Bluff Pass**  
A double-cut sequence that looks like a complete and fair cut of the deck. See [The Bluff Pass](bluff-pass.md).

**The Logical Control**  
A control using an apparently fair overhand shuffle with a logical justification for the handling built into the routine.

**The Spread Crimp**  
A card is crimped during a spread, allowing it to be cut to easily later. See [The Crimp](crimp.md).

**The Bridge Control**  
A bridge (a concave bow in the deck) placed by the performer marks the position of a returned card.

## Transfer Cuts as Controls (CC1 Ch. 6)

The double cut and triple cut deserve special mention here. These are technically false cuts (they restore the deck to its original order), but they are used primarily to bring a card to the top after it has been controlled to the bottom. They are covered in [False Cuts](false-cuts.md) but function as the second stage of many control sequences.

---

## What the Sources Agree On

All volumes agree that:
1. **The control must be invisible** — if the spectator thinks anything happened, the trick is damaged
2. **The shuffle or cut after replacement is the natural cover** — performing some handling immediately after card return looks fair
3. **No single control is universally best** — different situations require different methods

---

## The Ongoing Debate: Control vs. Force

A philosophical point Giobbi raises throughout the series: in many situations, a well-executed force is cleaner than a control, because no card ever needs to be found. The control is necessary when the selected card must be a genuinely free choice. When the effect permits a force, the force often produces a more powerful result.
