# Packet Switches

**Sources:** CC4 Ch. 41 (pp. 837–853)

---

## What Packet Switches Are

A **packet switch** secretly exchanges one group of cards for another group. Rather than switching a single card (see [Card Switches](card-switches.md)), the performer exchanges an entire small packet — typically three to six cards — for a different packet.

Packet switches are used in multi-phase effects, transpositions, and productions where the mechanical challenge would be impractical to solve with single-card techniques.

---

## The Veeser Concept

**Attributed to:** John Veeser

Rather than a specific switch, this is a **principle** or approach to packet switching. The Veeser Concept establishes that when two packets of equal size are held in the hands and then brought together, one packet can be substituted for the other if the geometry of the exchange is correct.

Giobbi describes this as the theoretical foundation underlying several of the specific switches in this chapter. The concept emphasizes that the **relationship between the two packets at the moment of exchange** is what determines the switch's invisibility, not the individual motions.

---

## The Toss Switch

One packet is apparently "tossed" from one hand to the other — and during the brief airborne moment or the catch, the switch occurs.

---

## The Talazac Switch

**Attributed to:** Talazac

A specific handling that exchanges two packets during an apparently normal transfer from one hand to the other.

---

## The Gamblers' Packet Switch

A switch developed from gambling technique — the exchange happens under the cover of dealing or spreading cards on the table.

---

## The Jinx Change

**Named for:** The Jinx magazine

A packet is placed on the table, and while another packet is tabled alongside it, the first packet has been switched.

---

## The Pendulum Switch

The hands make a pendulum-like motion — swinging toward and away from the body — and during the forward swing, the exchange occurs.

---

## Olram's Jinx Switch

**"Olram" = "Marlo" reversed. Attributed to:** Edward Marlo

A specific application of the Jinx Change idea with Marlo's mechanical refinements.

---

## The Turnover Palm Switch

A switch combined with a palming action — one packet is palmed while a second packet is deposited, or vice versa.

---

## The Steamboat Switch

A large-motion switch in which the hands move in an arc — the "steamboat" gesture — and the exchange happens during the arc's execution.

---

## The Count Switch

A switch that happens during an apparent counting of the packet — as cards are counted, a different packet is substituted.

---

## General Principles

Giobbi notes several principles that apply across all packet switches:

1. **Matching size matters:** Both packets should contain the same number of cards, or close to it, so that the exchange looks proportionally correct
2. **The exchange happens at a point of visual complexity:** During another action, during a moment when both hands are close together, or when attention is elsewhere
3. **Packet switches are often less clean than single-card switches** because there is more material to manage — extra attention to the handling before and after the switch is required
