# The Multiple Shift

**Sources:** CC3 Ch. 34 (pp. 661–679)

---

## What the Multiple Shift Is

The **Multiple Shift** (also called the Multiple Pass or the Card Transfer) moves several cards from different positions in the deck to a single destination — usually the top — in one action. It solves the problem of bringing multiple selected cards together when they have been scattered through the deck.

In a trick where three or four spectators each insert their selection into different parts of the deck, the Multiple Shift brings all of them together at the top instantly.

---

## The Insertion of the Cards

Before the shift can be executed, the cards must be inserted in a specific way that allows the shift to work. Giobbi teaches how to have spectators insert cards at different depths while the performer maintains a single little-finger break below all of them.

The standard setup: each card is inserted into the spread and a break is maintained below all insertions. The performer then closes the spread and executes the shift.

---

## The Versions

### Swing Cut Multiple Shift

The multiple cards are brought to the top during a swing cut action. The cut provides cover; at its conclusion, all selected cards are on top.

### Bluff Multiple Shift

The multiple shift is performed as a "bluff" — appearing to be two or three fair cuts, but actually functioning as a shift.

### The Vernon Multiple Shift

**Attributed to:** Dai Vernon

Vernon's version of the multiple shift using a specific hand position and weight distribution that makes the move feel (to the performer) more like a natural squaring than a sleight. This is considered the cleanest handling.

### Cardini's Overhand Multiple Shift

**Attributed to:** Cardini (Richard Valentine Pitchford)

A version executed during an overhand shuffle. As cards are shuffled, the inserted selections are brought together at the top. The overhand shuffle provides extended cover and looks more casual than the swing cut version.

### Carney's Natural Multiple Shift

**Attributed to:** John Carney

A handling developed by American card worker John Carney that emphasizes extreme naturalness — the shift looks like a genuinely casual squaring of the deck.

### The Simple Shift

A streamlined version for fewer cards (two or three) in less complex situations.

### The Tabled Multiple Shift

A version performed on the table — the deck is placed down and the shift occurs during the tabling and squaring action.

---

## Attribution Notes

The multiple shift has a long history in card magic, with versions attributed to S.W. Erdnase, Dai Vernon, and others. The specific named versions above are Giobbi's catalog of the most practically useful modern versions. Giobbi attributes each clearly:

- **Vernon Multiple Shift:** Dai Vernon
- **Cardini's version:** Cardini — the British-born comedy magician famous for his manipulative act
- **Carney's version:** John Carney, a modern American performer known for naturalistic technique
