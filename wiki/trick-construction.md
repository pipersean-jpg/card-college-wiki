# Construction of a Trick

**Sources:** CC2 Ch. 27 (pp. 426–432); CC4 Ch. 54 (pp. 1057–1068)

---

## What Construction Is

**Construction** describes how all the elements of a trick are assembled — not just the sleights, but the phases, the presentation, the narrative, and the choice of methods — into a unified experience that produces astonishment.

Giobbi treats construction as a structural science with specific principles, not as an intuitive art. Once these principles are understood, the performer can analyze and improve any trick systematically.

---

## The Construction of a Single Trick (CC2 Ch. 27)

Every card trick has an underlying **structure**. The structure determines the spectators' experience. Key structural elements:

**Setup:** Everything before the apparent magic begins. The deck is examined, selections are made, the situation is established.

**The Magical Effect:** The moment something impossible happens. This is the climax of the trick.

**Consistency:** Every step of the trick should appear internally logical. If the trick requires a card to be shuffled in, there should be a reason it was taken out in the first place. Giobbi defines **discrepancies** — inconsistencies or detours required by the method that don't appear in the "genuine" version of the trick — and argues they must be eliminated or justified.

**Climax Placement:** The magical effect should come as close to the end of the trick as possible. Anything that happens *after* the magic moment is "coming down" — the audience is already processing the impact. Extended denouements dilute the climax.

---

## Construction of a Routine

When multiple tricks are assembled into a routine, additional structural concerns arise:

**Variety of effect:** Each trick should produce a different *type* of magical experience — not all card-at-number, not all card-finding. Variety of effect keeps the audience engaged.

**Variety of method:** From the performer's perspective, different methods should be used in sequence so that multiple tricks cannot be explained by a single "theory" (e.g., "they're just looking at the bottom card each time").

**Build:** The routine should build in impact toward its strongest effect. The opener establishes rapport; the middle builds engagement; the closer delivers the most powerful experience.

**Transitions:** How one trick ends and the next begins. Smooth transitions feel like a unified program; awkward ones feel like a list.

## The Magic Pyramid (CC4 Ch. 54)

Giobbi's model for the hierarchical structure of magic. The pyramid has four levels:

1. **The Effect** (top): What the audience experiences — the impossible event
2. **The Technique** (second): The sleights and methods that produce the effect
3. **The Handling** (third): How the technique is managed in performance — angles, timing, rhythm
4. **The Management** (bottom/foundation): The overall choreography of the performance — what comes before and after, how spectators are positioned, how the performer moves

Most beginners work only at the technique level. Professionals work simultaneously at all four levels.

## The Magic Iceberg

A related concept Giobbi introduces in CC3 and develops in CC4: only a fraction of the performer's knowledge and preparation should be visible to the audience. The visible performance is the tip of the iceberg; below it lies the entire structure of methods, theory, and preparation.

The significance: the audience's experience of a "simple" trick is sustained by enormous invisible preparation. A trick that appears to happen naturally and without effort requires the most preparation of all — because all the effort must be hidden.

---

## Effects: Intellectual and Emotional (CC4 Ch. 54)

Giobbi distinguishes two types of magical effect:

**Intellectual effects:** The audience is challenged to figure out how the trick was done. These produce engagement and puzzle-solving pleasure, but do not produce deep astonishment. The audience is in their head.

**Emotional effects:** The audience feels something — wonder, delight, disorientation, connection. These produce lasting memory and the sense that something impossible happened.

The most powerful tricks combine both: they appear solvable (engaging the intellect) but are ultimately impossible to explain (triggering the emotional response).

---

## Dai Vernon's Criterion

Giobbi quotes Dai Vernon's practical construction test: *"Perform only tricks that you would do if you really could do magic."*

This is a simple but profound filter. If the performer possessed genuine magical powers, would they perform the trick exactly as structured? If not — if the method forces detours, complications, or justifications that a real magician wouldn't need — those detours must be either eliminated or made narratively invisible.

---

## Choosing Effects for the Program (CC2 Ch. 27)

Five tests Giobbi gives for evaluating whether a trick belongs in a performance:

1. **Is it good magic?** — Does it produce genuine astonishment?
2. **Is it within your ability?** — Can you execute it consistently?
3. **Is it well-constructed?** — Would a genuine magician do it this way?
4. **Does it serve the program?** — Does it fit the theme and sequence?
5. **Is this the best method?** — Have you researched all available methods?
