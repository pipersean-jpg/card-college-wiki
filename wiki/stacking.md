# Stacking

**Sources:** CC4 Ch. 45 (pp. 913–927)

---

## What Stacking Is

**Stacking** is the art of arranging cards in a specific, known order through shuffling or dealing — so that when the deck is dealt out, specific cards arrive at specific positions. The stacked deck looks shuffled and prepared randomly, but the performer knows exactly where each card will appear.

Stacking is the foundation of most serious gambling demonstrations and of certain "mathematical" card effects. It is the difference between having a lucky arrangement and engineering one.

---

## When Stacking Is Used

- **Gambling demonstrations:** The performer stacks the deck during "shuffling" before dealing, ensuring a winning hand at a predetermined position
- **Set-up tricks:** The trick requires cards to be at specific positions; the performer builds the setup covertly through shuffle-stacking
- **Multi-card productions:** Four aces, for example, can be stacked to arrive at positions 1, 2, 3, 4 from the top

---

## The Erdnase Stack Shuffle

**Attributed to:** S.W. Erdnase

The original shuffle-stacking technique from *The Expert at the Card Table*. A specific overhand shuffle sequence that moves cards to known positions through a precise series of runs and drops. Erdnase documented this as the cheater's method for building a stacked hand during normal shuffling.

Giobbi teaches the Erdnase Stack Shuffle as the foundational method and the historical baseline from which modern refinements depart.

---

## The Milk-build Shuffle

A method of gathering specific cards to the top of the deck in an alternating shuffle sequence — the shuffle "milks" the deck by taking alternately from top and bottom into a growing packet. With the right setup and sequence, this builds a specific top stock.

---

## The Frank Lane Stack

**Attributed to:** Frank Lane

A stacking approach developed by Frank Lane — a specific sequence for dealing-based stacking rather than shuffle-based. Cards are dealt in a controlled manner that arranges the top of the remaining deck.

---

## Riffle Shuffle Stacking (CC4 Ch. 45)

Stacking through riffle shuffles is more deceptive than overhand stacking because riffle shuffles appear more thorough. The technique requires:

1. Pre-counting the deck to know the current positions of target cards
2. Riffling in specific ways that move target cards to predetermined positions
3. Repeating this over several shuffles

This is considered among the most difficult techniques in the book — requiring both mechanical skill (the riffle shuffle must be genuinely clean) and mathematical awareness (knowing where each shuffle brings target cards). Giobbi notes this is the method used by the most sophisticated card cheats.

---

## Final Observations (CC4 Ch. 45)

Giobbi is candid: stacking through shuffling is extremely difficult and requires thousands of hours of practice to be usable under real conditions. Most performers who use stacking in their acts use pre-arranged decks (setup before the performance) rather than in-performance stacking.

The value of learning the shuffle-stacking technique is not necessarily for live use but for understanding the mechanics — which informs both the gambling demonstration context and the performer's overall deck awareness.
