# The Zarrow Shuffle

**Sources:** CC3 Ch. 33 (pp. 629–636)

**Originator:** Herb Zarrow

---

## What This Is

The Zarrow Shuffle is a tabled riffle false shuffle that preserves the entire deck in its original order while looking indistinguishable from a genuine dovetail shuffle. It is widely regarded as the most deceptive tabled false shuffle in existence and is a standard tool in the working card magician's repertoire.

Giobbi precedes the technical instruction with a discussion of "The Zarrow Dynamic" — his term for understanding *why* the shuffle works psychologically before learning *how* it works mechanically.

---

## The Zarrow Dynamic (CC3 Ch. 33)

Giobbi frames this section philosophically: a sleight is most deceptive when it looks *more* normal than normal. The Zarrow Shuffle achieves this because the squaring action at the end — which is where the deck is secretly restored — looks extremely natural. Spectators have been conditioned to expect a squaring action after a riffle shuffle. The Zarrow exploits this conditioning.

The key insight: the audience does not need to be fooled during the interleave. They are fooled during the squaring, which is the moment no one scrutinizes because it is perceived as a non-event.

---

## Technical Description

1. The deck is split into two approximately equal halves
2. The halves are riffled together in a normal dovetail — genuinely interleaved with cards alternating from each packet
3. As the hands bring the packets together to square them, the left hand pushes its interlocked packet *under* the right hand's packet (rather than squaring both packets flush)
4. The right hand's packet is simultaneously pulled *over* the left — creating a situation where one entire half lies below the other, separated by a thin layer of interlocked corners
5. The squaring motion presses the two halves flat, and the interlocked corners are pressed back into their respective halves
6. The deck is squared — and completely restored to its original order

The "Zarrow action" is entirely concealed by the natural-looking squaring motion.

---

## The Direct Zarrow Shuffle

A handling variation where the deck is not split into two separate packets before the shuffle. Instead, the performer acts as though splitting the deck but then directly executes the Zarrow action. This is cleaner in some performance contexts but is mechanically more challenging than the standard version.

---

## What Makes It Difficult

The shuffle is difficult not in the action itself but in **the feel**: the interlocked corners must release cleanly when the squaring motion is applied. If they catch, they produce a visible buckle or an audible click. This requires:

1. Cards in good condition (not worn or soft)
2. Precisely calibrated pressure during the interleave — not too deep
3. Practice until the release is smooth and silent

Giobbi recommends extensive practice on an actual close-up pad to develop the feel, as the surface beneath the deck affects how the corners release.

---

## Zarrow vs. Pull-Through: The Ongoing Debate

In teaching circles, there is ongoing debate about whether the Zarrow Shuffle (tabled) or the Pull-Through Shuffle (in-the-hands) is superior.

**Arguments for the Zarrow:**
- Performed on the table, it is more deceptive to people sitting across from the performer
- The tabled action looks more casual and less controlled than an in-the-hands shuffle
- Close-up workers who sit at tables find it more natural

**Arguments for the Pull-Through:**
- Standing performers cannot use the Zarrow without a table
- The Pull-Through is valid from more angles
- Some argue the sound of the interleave in the Zarrow is slightly unusual to trained ears

Giobbi teaches both and leaves the choice to the performer based on their performance conditions. He notes that for close-up workers who primarily perform at a table, the Zarrow is the gold standard.

## Attribution

The shuffle is universally attributed to **Herb Zarrow**, an American card worker who created it in the mid-twentieth century. Giobbi credits Zarrow explicitly and introduces the method in the context of Zarrow's contribution to the field.
