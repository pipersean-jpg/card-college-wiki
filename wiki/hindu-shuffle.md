# The Hindu Shuffle

**Sources:** CC1 Ch. 11 (pp. 153–165)

---

## What the Hindu Shuffle Is

The **Hindu Shuffle** is a style of shuffling cards originating in South Asia, widely used throughout Asia and recognized in many parts of the world. The right hand holds the deck by the sides (not the ends) and lifts it; the left thumb and fingers grasp small packets from the top and let them fall into the left palm repeatedly, until the deck is exhausted.

Visually, the Hindu Shuffle looks completely different from the overhand or riffle shuffle — cards fall in small clumps downward rather than being peeled off the top. For audiences familiar with it, it reads as a natural shuffle.

---

## Technique

The deck starts in the right hand, held by the sides between thumb and fingers. The left hand approaches from below. The left thumb and second finger contact the top packet; the right hand drops away slightly, releasing that top packet into the left hand. The left hand accepts the packet. The right hand moves to take the next packet. This continues until all packets have fallen into the left hand.

The shuffle is completely genuine in its basic form — it produces real mixing.

---

## Placing a Key Card with a Hindu Shuffle

By controlling when the left hand accepts or releases its packet, the performer can place a key card at any desired position. This is the first control taught with the Hindu Shuffle.

---

## Glimpsing the Bottom Card with a Hindu Shuffle

As the last packet falls into the left hand during a genuine shuffle, the bottom card of that final packet can be briefly seen by the performer (glimpsed) before the deck is squared. This provides a key card without any overt preparation.

---

## The Hindu Shuffle Force

One of the most versatile forces in card magic:

1. The force card is on the bottom of the deck
2. The performer begins a genuine Hindu Shuffle
3. The spectator is asked to "call stop" at any point
4. When the spectator says stop, the performer extends the remaining packet in the right hand — the bottom card (the force card) is now the face of this packet
5. "Is this your card?" — The spectator appears to have stopped the shuffle freely

The Hindu Shuffle Force has a high success rate and is deceptive because the stopping point is genuinely free and the force card is genuinely at whatever position the spectator stopped. The only condition: the force card must be on the bottom and the performer must continue shuffling until after the spectator says stop.

---

## Hindu Shuffle Card Controls

### Controlling a Single Card

A card is returned to the deck as packets are being dropped. The performer lifts the right hand's remaining packet and the returned card is now between packets in a controlled position. A final shuffle run maneuvers it to the top.

### Controlling Multiple Cards

The same concept extended — multiple cards returned to the deck are kept as a group and shuttled to the top.

---

## Cultural Context

Giobbi notes that the Hindu Shuffle's cultural origin affects its reception in performance. For non-Western audiences, it may look completely natural. For Western audiences, it may look unusual or foreign — which can either add interest ("how do you do that?") or arouse suspicion ("why is he doing it that way?"). The performer should choose their shuffle style based on their audience.
