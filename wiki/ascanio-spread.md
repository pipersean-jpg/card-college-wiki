# The Ascanio Spread

**Sources:** CC3 Ch. 32 (pp. 595–602)

**Originator:** Arturo de Ascanio

---

## What This Is

The Ascanio Spread is a display technique for apparently showing all the cards of a small packet while secretly hiding one or more cards. Cards are spread fan-like between the hands with their faces toward the audience — yet a hidden card (or cards) nestles invisibly within the spread, shielded by the geometry of the fan.

It is named for its creator, the Spanish magician **Arturo de Ascanio**, who was one of the most important card theorists of the 20th century and to whom Giobbi dedicated CC3.

---

## How It Works

The packet is held face-up in the left hand. As the right hand spreads the cards from the left, one card (the hidden one) is held back against the left hand's fingers. The spread fans outward from the face card, and the audience sees what appears to be all the cards in an open display.

The hidden card stays in register with the card above it in the spread — their alignment is such that the hidden card is completely shielded by the card above it. Because the spread is in motion (spreading and then closing), spectators never have a static angle from which to see the card that isn't there.

---

## The Ascanio Spread Fan

A variation in which the spread is completed into a fan shape — the hidden card is concealed in the closed portion of the fan near the left thumb. This is useful when a wider display is needed.

---

## The Open Display

A related display Giobbi develops in CC3: the packet is spread openly in the right hand, showing all faces apparently, while a card is hidden in the left hand's holding position. This is related to the Ascanio Spread in principle but is used in different contexts.

---

## Why This Is Important

The Ascanio Spread solves a problem that counting methods like the Elmsley Count do not: **static displays**. A count shows cards while they are in motion; once they stop moving, the audience can look at them. The Ascanio Spread allows a performer to *pause* with the spread open and invite inspection — the hidden card is still concealed in the static display.

This is why the Ascanio Spread is the preferred display method in European card magic traditions, where Ascanio's influence was profound. Giobbi uses it extensively in his tricks and credits it as one of Ascanio's most important contributions.

---

## Ascanio's Broader Theory of Displays

Giobbi notes that Ascanio's contribution was not just the specific spread but an entire *philosophy* of displaying cards honestly while concealing their true composition. Ascanio's insight was that **the audience infers completeness from the display's *feeling* of openness**, not from counting individual cards. If the spread looks natural and free, spectators do not count — they simply feel that all the cards have been seen.

This philosophy — that perception can be managed through design rather than speed — influenced Giobbi deeply and permeates CC3's theory sections.
