# Other Forces

**Sources:** CC1 Ch. 5 (pp. 83–92); CC4 Ch. 39 (pp. 791–804)

---

## Overview

Beyond the Classic Force and Riffle Force, Giobbi catalogues a large repertoire of specific force methods throughout the series. This article surveys the less central but practically important forces.

---

## Volume 1 Forces (Chapter 5)

### The Crisscross Force (Balducci Force / Cross-cut Force)

**Attributed to:** Ed Balducci

The spectator cuts the deck freely and places the top packet crosswise on the bottom packet. After a brief conversational pause, the performer lifts the top (crosswise) packet and points to the top card of the lower packet as "the card at the cut." This card is, of course, the original top card of the deck before the cut.

The force works entirely through **time displacement**: the brief pause and conversation cause the spectator to forget the original positions of the two packets. They believe the "card at the cut" was randomly determined by their own cut.

**Caution:** This force is widely known in the magic world and in the general public. Giobbi advises using it judiciously and never repeating it for the same audience.

### The Goldin Force

**Attributed to:** Goldin

A spread-based force that uses a specific mechanical advantage to position the force card. Less detail is given by Giobbi; he presents it as one of several spread-type forces.

### The Ten-to-twenty Force

A mathematical force that requires no physical dexterity:
1. Ask the spectator to think of a number between 10 and 20
2. Deal that many cards face down
3. Add the two digits of their number (e.g., 17 → 1+7 = 8)
4. Count down that many from the dealt pile
5. The card at that position is the force card

This works because any number from 10 to 20 produces a digit sum between 1 and 11, and the resulting final position always lands on the same card (the 10th card from the original deck top, if set up correctly).

A completely automatic force requiring no dexterity. Its weakness: the spectator *watches* you deal cards, which can feel procedural and mechanical in a way that undermines the atmosphere of a magic performance.

## Volume 4 Forces (Chapter 39)

### The Under-the-Table Force

A force performed with the deck beneath the table, out of sight. The spectator names a number; the performer counts down to that card under the table. The force relies on a specific setup and the performer's ability to count cards by feel.

### The Ribbon-spread Force

The deck is ribbon-spread face down across the table. The spectator touches or slides out a card. Through a specific set-up positioning, the card they most naturally pick up is the force card.

### The Toss Force

The deck is tossed or fanned, and the spectator takes a card in the air or from the fan. The force relies on controlling which card is accessible at the moment the spectator reaches.

### The Peek Force

The spectator "peeks" at a card by pushing the corner open during a riffle. The performer forces the peek position — the card at the peek point is the force card.

### The Cut Force

The spectator cuts the deck and the cut is used to force the position. Related to the Crisscross Force but using a different follow-through.

### The Credit-card Force

The spectator uses a credit card or business card to stop the performer's riffle. The credit card's edge is used to establish the stop point, which the performer controls.

### The Curious Count

A mathematical force based on a counting sequence with a specific setup.

### The Packet Force

A force performed with a small packet of cards rather than the full deck.

### The Mishmash Force

A compound force combining elements of spreading, dealing, and audience interaction.

### Forcing Under a Handkerchief

The force is performed with the deck covered by a handkerchief. A theatrical and unusual approach that adds a layer of impossibility.

### The Bottom Bluff Force

The force card is on the bottom of the deck. A specific handling bluffs the spectator into believing they are selecting from the middle.

### The Dribble Force

Cards are dribbled from one hand to the other and the spectator "stops" the dribble. The force card is positioned to be at the top of the dribbled packet at the moment the spectator most naturally stops.

---

## Organizing Principles

Giobbi doesn't rank these forces hierarchically; instead he notes that each is optimal in specific conditions:

- **Large audience or group:** Ribbon-spread and tabled forces work better; hand-to-hand forces have angle problems
- **Skeptical audience:** Mathematical forces (Ten-to-twenty, Curious Count) remove any apparent skill, making them harder to attribute to technique
- **Theater setting:** Under-the-table and handkerchief forces add theatrical atmosphere
- **Casual intimate setting:** The Classic Force and Riffle Force remain the gold standard
