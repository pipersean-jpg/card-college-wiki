# Arturo de Ascanio

**Full name:** Arturo de Ascanio y Martínez (1925–1997)  
**Nationality:** Spanish  
**Based in:** Madrid

---

## Who He Was

Arturo de Ascanio was a Spanish magician, theorist, and close-up card worker who became one of the most important figures in twentieth-century European magic. He was a founding intellectual presence of the **Escuela Mágica de Madrid** (Magical School of Madrid) — an informal group of Spanish magicians whose approach to magic theory and performance influenced practitioners worldwide.

Giobbi's connection to Ascanio is deep and personal. *Card College Volume 1* is dedicated, among others, to Ascanio — with the description "the scholarly Arturo de Ascanio for his intellectual brilliance and paternal care." *Card College Volume 3* carries a poem by Ascanio as its dedication page, and Giobbi notes in a moving footnote that Ascanio died on April 6, 1997, while discussing magic with a fellow magician — a deck of cards in his hands.

---

## Techniques Attributed to Ascanio

**The Ascanio Spread** (CC3 Ch. 32) — Ascanio's most widely known contribution to card technique. A display method for showing a small packet of cards while secretly concealing one or more. See [The Ascanio Spread](../ascanio-spread.md).

**The Ascanio Spread Fan** — A fan-based variant of the same principle.

Giobbi attributes these specifically to Ascanio and discusses them in the context of his broader theoretical contribution.

---

## Ascanio's Theoretical Contributions

Ascanio's influence extended far beyond individual techniques. His primary contribution was a rigorous **theory of deception in card magic**:

**The Principle of Genuine-looking Deception:** Ascanio articulated that the most deceptive moments in magic are not those where something is hidden, but those where the audience *feels* they are seeing everything openly. His insight: perception of openness and completeness can be engineered.

**The Display Philosophy:** Ascanio's approach to displays (spreading, showing, fanning) was that the *feeling* of a complete, honest display is what matters — not whether every card is literally shown. If the spread feels open and natural, spectators will not count. The Ascanio Spread is the practical embodiment of this philosophy.

**Ascanio's Teaching:** He was known among Spanish magicians for his intellectual rigor and willingness to analyze magic systematically. The Escuela Mágica de Madrid developed a culture of serious theoretical study that Giobbi absorbed and reflects throughout Card College.

---

## Giobbi's Personal Tribute

Giobbi writes in CC3 that Ascanio was a paternal figure — someone whose intellectual approach to magic and whose personal warmth shaped Giobbi's own development as both a performer and a teacher. The dedication poem, written by Ascanio, reflects a deeply personal relationship between teacher and student in the best sense.

The footnote noting Ascanio's death — "with a deck of cards in his hands" — is one of the most moving moments in the entire five-volume series.
