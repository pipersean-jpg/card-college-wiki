# Edward Marlo

**Full name:** Edward Marlo (1913–1991)  
**Nationality:** American  
**Based in:** Chicago, Illinois

---

## Who He Was

Edward Marlo was the most prolific creator of card sleights in the history of magic. A Chicago native who worked as a professional card player and later devoted himself entirely to magic creation, Marlo published an estimated ten thousand or more techniques over his lifetime through a continuous stream of booklets, manuscripts, and magazine contributions.

His approach was different from Vernon's: where Vernon favored perfecting a small number of techniques to absolute naturalness, Marlo's passion was *invention* — finding new mechanical solutions to problems, exploring variations, and pushing the technical boundaries of what was possible with cards.

---

## Techniques Attributed to Marlo in Giobbi's Series

**Tilt** (CC4 Ch. 50): Marlo's most widely-used technique — a method for apparently inserting a card into the middle of the deck while actually placing it second from the top. See [Tilt](../tilt.md).

**The Olram Subtlety** (CC3 Ch. 32): "Olram" is "Marlo" spelled backwards — a convention used specifically for Marlo attributions in magic literature. The Olram Subtlety is a handling refinement applied to the Elmsley Count that makes it more convincing. See [False Counts and Displays](../false-counts.md).

**Olram's Jinx Switch** (CC4 Ch. 41): A packet switch technique. See [Packet Switches](../packet-switches.md).

## The "Olram" Convention

The use of "Olram" (Marlo reversed) as a pseudonymous attribution is a deliberate signal in magic literature: when you see "Olram," it means Marlo. The reason for the reversal is partly convention (Marlo reportedly used the pseudonym in some publications) and partly a marker in the literature that helps readers recognize the source.

Giobbi uses this convention correctly throughout the series.

---

## Marlo's Influence on Modern Card Technique

Marlo's influence is pervasive in modern card magic, even when his name is not explicitly cited. Many of the techniques now treated as "standard" — including various second deals, passes, double lifts, and packet sleights — were either invented or significantly refined by Marlo.

His publication method (constant small booklets and manuscripts) meant that his ideas circulated widely among card workers during his lifetime, and many techniques were absorbed into the general card magic vocabulary before their Marlo attribution was widely recognized.

---

## Marlo vs. Vernon: A Philosophical Debate

The broader magic community has often positioned Marlo and Vernon as representing two philosophical poles:

- **Vernon:** Fewer techniques, deeply mastered, performance-focused, naturalness above all
- **Marlo:** Vast technical invention, mechanically creative, interested in the problem of technique for its own sake

Giobbi clearly respects both traditions. His series teaches many Marlo techniques (Tilt, the Olram Subtlety) while structuring the overall course around Vernonian principles of quality and natural performance.
