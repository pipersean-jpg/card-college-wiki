# Fundamental Techniques

**Sources:** CC1 Ch. 1 (pp. 13–36); CC1 Introduction

---

## What This Covers

This article covers the non-sleight handling skills that every card worker must own before any sleight makes sense: squaring the deck, spreading cards, dribbling, dealing, and the ribbon spread. These techniques have no deception in themselves — their purpose is to make the performer's handling look fluid, natural, and professional at all times.

Giobbi opens *Card College* with these basics not as a formality but as a philosophical statement: **the baseline of handling must look identical to the sleight**. If your double lift looks different from a normal turnover, the audience will sense it even if they cannot name it. The fundamentals set the standard against which all sleights are measured.

---

## Squaring the Deck

The deck must be kept perfectly squared at all times except when deliberately spread or fanned. Giobbi teaches the **All-around Square-up**: the right hand approaches from above and the fingers and thumb simultaneously tamp all four sides of the deck into alignment. This full-perimeter squaring looks thorough and natural, and it doubles as a cover for various break acquisitions and card placements.

Sloppy squaring is a chronic fault in amateur card workers. An unsquared deck invites spectators to wonder what is between the cards.

## Spreading Cards Between the Hands

Cards are spread from the left hand into the right by pushing them with the left thumb while the right fingers receive them and space them evenly. This hand-to-hand spread is used constantly for displaying cards and for allowing a spectator to select one.

**Outjogging cards during a spread:** As cards are spread, specific cards can be pushed inward or outward by the left thumb without breaking the rhythm of the spread. This is the basis of the spread cull and many other techniques.

---

## Dribbling Cards

Cards are released from the deck in a continuous stream from right hand to left (or onto the table). The right thumb releases cards from the inner end while the fingers provide resistance. Dribbling serves as:

- A fair-seeming way to let a spectator "call stop"
- Cover for the dribble break
- A component of the dribble force and dribble pass
- A visual display of handling ease

---

## Dealing Cards

Two dealing methods are established early:

- **Draw method (face down):** The left thumb pushes the top card to the right, and the right hand takes it by its right edge and deals it face down — the standard poker deal
- **Stud method (face up):** The right hand takes the card face down, then flips it face up as it is placed on the table

The distinction matters because different tricks use different dealing styles, and performing the wrong one mid-trick creates visual inconsistency.

---

## The Ribbon Spread

Cards are spread in a smooth, even line across a close-up pad or table. A clean ribbon spread requires:

1. Placing the deck at the left end of the pad at a 45° angle to the line of travel
2. Dragging rightward while applying even downward pressure
3. Keeping spacing uniform throughout

The ribbon spread is used to display a full deck, to allow free selection, to table a card face down for later revelation, and as part of the ribbon spread turnover (where all cards are flipped face up in a wave — a flourish taught in CC1 Ch. 12).

---

## The Close-up Pad

Giobbi advocates strongly for using a close-up pad (a felt or velvet mat). The pad:

- Allows the ribbon spread to work smoothly
- Gives the hands a consistent work surface
- Prevents cards from sliding
- Looks professional

This is one of the few instances where Giobbi recommends a specific prop beyond the cards themselves.

## A Brief History of Playing Cards (CC1 pp. 5–7)

Giobbi opens with historical context: playing cards likely originated in China in the 9th century CE, migrated through Persia and Egypt, and arrived in Europe by the late 14th century. The four-suit system (hearts, diamonds, clubs, spades) is a French innovation from around 1480. Modern poker-size cards (2.5" × 3.5") became the standard for sleight of hand in the 20th century.

Understanding the history gives the card worker a richer connection to the tradition they are participating in — and provides material for presentations.
