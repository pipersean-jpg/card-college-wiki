# The Double Lift

**Sources:** CC1 Ch. 9 (pp. 127–134); CC2 Ch. 21 (pp. 329–343); CC3 Ch. 31 (pp. 571–592)

---

## What the Double Lift Is

The **Double Lift** is one of the most important and frequently-used sleights in card magic. It involves picking up the top *two* cards of the deck and handling them as if they were one card, so the second card from the top appears to be the top card.

Its applications are vast:
- Show a card that is not the card (the performer's secret card is hidden behind the displayed one)
- Switch a card — the audience sees "their card" being turned face up, placed aside, or handed to them, but actually receives a different card
- Set up for numerous subsequent revelations and exchanges

---

## The Double Lift and the Double Turnover

**Double Lift:** Picking up two cards as one and holding them in the right hand

**Double Turnover:** Turning two cards as one face up (or face down) on top of the deck, without lifting them away

The Double Turnover is generally more practical and natural-looking in most situations — the cards never leave the deck, and the turnover action is identical to turning over the top card normally.

## Volume 1, Chapter 9 — The Basic Double Lift

### Getting Ready (The Get-ready)

The fundamental get-ready: a little-finger break is obtained beneath the top two cards, so that when the right hand reaches to turn over the top card, it actually grips two cards as one.

The break can be obtained by:
- The left thumb counting two cards downward as it rests on the top of the deck
- A small push-over of the top card to create a step
- During any natural squaring action

### A Double Lift

The right hand picks up two cards as one from the top, held by the ends. The pair is displayed as a single card, then replaced face down on top or placed elsewhere.

### A Double Turnover

The right thumb and fingers grip the two cards at the inner end and flip them over face-up on top of the deck (or face-down back to their starting position). The motion looks identical to turning over a single top card.

**Critical detail:** The outer long edges of the two cards must be perfectly aligned — any misalignment (called "getting out of alignment" or producing a "tell") is the primary failure mode of the double turnover. The cards must look and feel as one.

---

## Volume 2, Chapter 21 — Another Double Turnover

### Another Double Turnover

A different get-ready: the top two cards are pushed over together as a unit by the left thumb — the push-over double. The right hand grips and flips them from their pushed-over position.

This is in many ways the most elegant approach: the push-over makes getting the two cards as one very clean, and the flip is natural. It is the version used in most subsequent techniques where a double lift is required.

## Volume 3, Chapter 31 — Advanced Techniques

### The Push-over Double

The definitive treatment of the push-over get-ready. The left thumb pushes two cards uniformly to the right so they may be gripped by the right thumb and index finger. Giobbi treats this in detail, including:
- The correct finger positions during the push
- How to ensure both cards align perfectly
- The pace of the push — slow enough to feel natural, not hesitant

### A Double Lift from the Center

A double lift executed on a packet at a position other than the top of the deck — used in specific multi-phase routines where the double lift must happen in the middle of a spread.

### Small Packet Double Lift

A double lift from a small packet of four to six cards — different handling required because the grip is different.

### Replacements (How to Return the Double Card)

Returning the two cards to the top face-down after displaying them face-up is as important as the lift itself. Giobbi teaches three methods:

**The Optical Replacement:** The two cards are replaced with a slight momentum that makes them "snap" flat onto the top — this optical snap looks like a single card being turned over naturally.

**The Turnover Replacement:** The two cards are replaced with a specific wrist turn that deposits them without exposing the alignment.

**The Vernon Replacement:** Attributed to **Dai Vernon**. A subtle replacement that appears to be the most casual and natural of the three, making it the preferred replacement for most performance contexts. The key element is the *angle* at which the two-card packet descends onto the deck — a specific diagonal approach that makes them appear to land as one.

### A Double Lift Finesse

A specific handling refinement that allows the double lift to be performed in a more natural way when the standard get-ready would look awkward in context.

## Common Double Lift Errors

Giobbi identifies the most common failure modes across all three teaching chapters:

1. **Visible break:** The break under two cards produces a visible gap before the lift
2. **Misaligned cards:** The two cards do not align perfectly at the outer edge, so the audience sees two cards instead of one
3. **Hesitation in the lift:** Pausing before taking the two cards signals that something is happening
4. **Different grip from single-card handling:** The right hand grips the double differently from how it normally picks up a card — this tells the audience something is different

The standard remedy: practice the double lift until it is handled *exactly* like a single card, at the same speed and with the same grip.

## The Double Lift Throughout the Series

The double lift appears in nearly every subsequent volume because it is the foundation of so many effects:

- The Ambitious Card routine (CC2) uses repeated double lifts
- Numerous sandwich effects use doubles to display the "same" card repeatedly
- The Double Lift Reverse (CC2 Ch. 24) combines a double with a reversal
- The Double Lift Glimpse (CC2 Ch. 23) uses the double to secretly see the second card

---

## Debate: Push-over vs. Break Get-ready

The question of whether to use a little-finger break or a push-over as the get-ready has been debated extensively in teaching circles.

**For the break:** The break is more versatile — it can be held for longer, and the lift can be delayed naturally.

**For the push-over:** The push-over produces better card alignment because the left thumb moves both cards uniformly; it is more consistent; and there is no break to accidentally display.

Giobbi teaches both and recommends the push-over as the primary method for the double turnover, noting that the break is better suited for the double lift (cards picked up from the deck). Many experienced performers use one method exclusively for consistency.
