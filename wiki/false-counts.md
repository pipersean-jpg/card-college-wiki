# False Counts and Displays

**Sources:** CC2 Ch. 20 (pp. 309–327); CC3 Ch. 32 (pp. 593–625)

---

## What False Counts Are

A **false count** is a counting action that displays a packet of cards while secretly misrepresenting the number of cards in the packet — showing four cards when there are really five, or showing four identical cards when one or more are different.

These techniques are used primarily in **packet tricks** — effects using small groups of cards (four to eight cards) rather than the full deck. The false count is the tool that lets the performer apparently display an honest count while concealing either extra cards or different cards.

---

## Why They Work

False counts exploit the **attention-to-action** principle: when a performer counts cards visibly, spectators track the action of counting (cards moving from hand to hand) but do not track every individual card carefully. If one card is held back or slipped underneath another, and the rhythm of the count remains steady, spectators register the count itself as honest.

The performer's job: make the rhythm and appearance of every card transfer look identical. Any change in rhythm signals a discrepancy.

## Volume 2, Chapter 20 — The Primary Counts

### The Elmsley Count

**Attributed to:** Alex Elmsley

The most important false count in packet magic. Starting with four cards (one of which is "odd" — different from the others), the performer counts them one by one into the right hand, apparently showing all four. However, the odd card is never shown — it passes under the growing packet in the right hand and is never seen.

The result: the audience believes all four cards were displayed and all are identical.

**Mechanics:** Cards are counted from left hand to right in a specific sequence where one card is held back by the right hand under the growing pile while the visible count continues with the outer cards. The count reads as: 1 – 2 – 3 – 4, but card three is the same card as card two (the odd card passed beneath it during card three's count).

The Elmsley Count is foundational and appears in hundreds of published packet tricks. It is the starting point for all subsequent false counts.

### The Jordan Count

**Attributed to:** Charles T. Jordan

A count that adds a card to a packet — showing four cards as five, or similar. The extra card is hidden under the others and secretly added during one of the counting actions.

### The Hamman Count

**Attributed to:** Brother John Hamman

Shows a packet of cards where one is secretly different. The mechanics differ from the Elmsley in the direction the odd card travels during the count — here it passes in the opposite direction, giving a slightly different visual rhythm that some audiences find harder to track.

### The Buckle Count

A count using a "buckle" — the bottom card of the packet is buckled (bent away) by the right finger, allowing a card to slip under it during the count. Used to show fewer cards than are present.

### The Push-off Count

Cards are pushed off the face-card packet one at a time with the thumb, and received into the right hand. The false element is introduced at a specific point in the push-off sequence.

### The Hamman Two-for-Four Count

**Attributed to:** Brother John Hamman

A count that shows two cards as four — valuable in packet effects where the performer wants the spectator to believe four identical cards are present, but the setup requires only two real cards plus two hidden.

## Volume 3, Chapter 32 — Advanced Displays

### The Ascanio Spread

**Attributed to:** Arturo de Ascanio

See the dedicated article: [The Ascanio Spread](ascanio-spread.md)

The most elegant false display in card magic. Not a "count" but a spread display — cards are fanned or spread to show apparently every card, while one or more hidden cards remain concealed.

### The Olram Subtlety

**"Olram" = "Marlo" reversed. Attributed to:** Edward Marlo

A specific handling subtlety applied to the Elmsley Count or similar counts: the packet is squared and handled in a specific way before the count that makes the count more convincing by resetting the audience's expectation.

### The Gemini Count

A count showing a packet where two cards are the same — displaying two "identical" cards while hiding that they are actually different.

### The Over-count

A count that displays more cards than are actually in the packet — showing four when there are only three.

### The Multiple-lift Display Sequence

A sequence combining multiple double lifts with counting and display actions, used in routines where cards must change or multiply.

### The Flushtration Count

A count where cards are transferred from one hand to the other with their backs toward the audience — only the backs are seen, never the faces. This hides the faces of any cards that must not be displayed.

### Displaying All the Cards as Identical

A technique for showing all cards of a packet (face-to-back, spread) while concealing that some are different.

### The D'Amico Spread

**Attributed to:** D'Amico

A spread technique that conceals one or more cards in the display — related to the Ascanio Spread but with different mechanics and different concealment geometry.

## The Critical Role of Rhythm

Giobbi emphasizes across both chapters that **rhythm is the key to all false counts**. Every card's transfer should look and feel the same as every other card's transfer. The fake moment — where a card is held back, added, or skipped — must happen at the same *pace* and with the same *sound* as a genuine transfer.

Practicing with a metronome, or simply counting aloud while executing the count, can help develop consistent rhythm.

---

## What the Sources Agree On

The Elmsley Count is universally cited as the foundational false count. The Hamman Count is nearly as important. The Ascanio Spread (for display rather than counting) is from a distinct European tradition — Ascanio's approach was spread-based rather than count-based, and Giobbi (who studied with Ascanio) gives it prominent treatment in CC3.

---

## Attribution Debate

The exact origins of several counts are disputed in the broader magic literature, though Giobbi's attributions are generally consistent with the mainstream historical view:
- The **Elmsley Count** is universally credited to **Alex Elmsley**, a British mathematician-magician
- The **Hamman Count** is universally credited to **Brother John Hamman**, an American magician-priest
- The Olram Subtlety being attributed to Marlo ("Olram" reversed) is the standard attribution and is noted explicitly by Giobbi
