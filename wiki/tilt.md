# Tilt

**Sources:** CC4 Ch. 50 (pp. 995–1011)

**Originator:** Edward Marlo

---

## What Tilt Is

**Tilt** is a sleight that allows a card to be visibly inserted into what appears to be the middle of the deck, while actually going second from the top.

The name comes from the action: the deck is tilted — angled downward at the front — creating an apparent gap in the middle. The spectator or the performer inserts a card into this apparent middle gap, but the geometry of the tilt means the card actually goes under only the top card.

---

## Why It Matters

Tilt solves a specific and important problem: **visible replacement**. In many tricks, the audience needs to see the selected card being placed back in the middle of the deck. With standard controls, the performer must lose the card and then secretly relocate it — but Tilt creates the *appearance* of losing the card in the middle while actually placing it near the top.

The result: the card is "lost" visibly yet is actually second from the top, ready to be turned over in a double lift, controlled to the top, or otherwise used immediately.

---

## Technical Description

The deck is held in left-hand dealing position. The right hand approaches from above and grips the deck.

1. The right hand tilts the front (outer) end of the deck downward by a few degrees
2. This tilt creates a visual gap between the top card and the rest of the deck — the angle makes it look like a deep gap in the middle
3. The card to be inserted is pushed into this apparent gap from the front
4. The card slides under only the top card — it is now second from the top
5. The deck is squared and everything looks normal

The deception works because spectators looking down at the deck from the outside see the perspective-distorted gap and interpret it as being in the middle. The tilt angle creates this illusion.

## Tilt Finesse I and II

Two refinements of the basic tilt:

**Finesse I:** A handling modification that makes the card insertion look more natural by adjusting the angle of the deck and the point at which the card enters.

**Finesse II:** A handling for specific situations where the standard Finesse I would be difficult (e.g., when the performer is sitting vs. standing, or when the spectator inserts their own card).

---

## Tilt in Routines

Tilt is a key technique in "Ambitious Card" routines — where a selected card repeatedly rises to the top of the deck despite being lost in the middle. Tilt allows the "losing" phase to be visible and apparently fair, while setting up the inevitable return.

CC4 includes the trick **Ultra-ambitious**, a complete Ambitious Card routine built primarily around Tilt.

---

## Attribution

The Tilt is universally attributed to **Edward Marlo**, the prolific Chicago card worker who published thousands of techniques. Giobbi credits Marlo explicitly and notes the technique's widespread influence on modern card magic.

---

## Debate: Tilt vs. Other Controls

Tilt is sometimes criticized because the angled deck position is a departure from normal handling — it requires the deck to be tilted, which looks slightly different from the flat dealing position. Some performers and teachers argue that a spectator who watches carefully will notice the tilt.

Giobbi acknowledges this and addresses it: the tilt position should be introduced before the crucial moment as part of natural handling, so that when the insertion occurs, the deck's angle has already been seen as normal.
