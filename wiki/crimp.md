# The Crimp

**Sources:** CC2 Ch. 22 (pp. 345–352); CC3 Ch. 30 (pp. 561–562)

---

## What the Crimp Is

A **crimp** is a small, deliberate bend placed in a card, creating a permanent-until-corrected physical deformation. This bend serves as a locator: when the deck is cut or riffled, the crimped card can be found by feel, and the deck can be cut to it reliably.

Unlike a break (which must be maintained with muscular effort), the crimp persists without the performer doing anything. The card can be cut to immediately, after a genuine shuffle, or even after being handled by spectators.

---

## Types of Crimp

### Crimping with the Index Finger

The right index finger presses down on one corner of a card during the squaring action. This creates a small diagonal bend at the corner — enough to feel, not enough to see from the front.

### Crimping with the Heel of the Thumb

The left thumb's heel presses down on the inner right corner of the top card while the deck is being squared. A slight downward pressure creates the crimp.

### Crimping with an Overhand Shuffle

During an overhand shuffle, a specific card's corner is pressed against the left palm as packets are dropped, creating a subtle bend.

---

## Cutting to a Crimped Card (CC2 Ch. 22)

Because the crimped card has a slight bow, the riffle of the deck's corner naturally breaks at the crimp. The thumb riffling the corner will "fall into" the crimp — releasing cards faster or slower at that point than surrounding cards. With practice, the performer can cut exactly to the crimp.

Applications:
- **Cutting to a spectator's card:** The spectator's selected card is crimped; after a genuine shuffle or cut, the performer cuts directly to it
- **Locator in shuffled decks:** A crimped card in a set-up deck survives shuffling (the bend travels through the shuffle) and remains findable

---

## The Spread Crimp (CC3 Ch. 30)

A more advanced application. During a spread of the deck, the performer crimps a specific card (the spectator's selection) using the pressure of the spread. This is particularly deceptive because the crimping action happens during the very same spread in which the card was returned — before the audience expects any preparation.

After the Spread Crimp, the deck can be genuinely shuffled and cut by the spectator, and the performer can still cut to the selection at will.

---

## Subtlety and Risk

The crimp's weakness: spectators examining a card may feel the crimp. It should be as small as possible — barely enough to function. Giobbi notes that a well-placed crimp is invisible to normal handling but detectable if someone deliberately bends and examines the cards.

For performance purposes, this risk is generally acceptable — spectators do not routinely bend and inspect individual cards. But it is a consideration in environments where a skeptical spectator might examine the deck.

---

## The Bridge

Related but distinct: a **bridge** is a bend applied to an *entire* packet, creating a convex or concave bow across multiple cards. Bridges are used in card table work to allow a cut to arrive at a specific position. Giobbi mentions bridges in the context of the Bridge Control (CC3 Ch. 30).
