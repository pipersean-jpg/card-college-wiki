# The Pass — Overview

**Sources:** CC2 Ch. 19 (pp. 295–308); CC4 Ch. 49 (pp. 969–992)

---

## What the Pass Is

The **Pass** (also called the Shift or the Classic Pass) is a sleight that secretly transposes the two halves of the deck in a single, apparently instantaneous action. A card controlled to the bottom of the top half through a break is brought to the top of the deck in one invisible movement.

It is universally regarded as the most powerful single sleight in card magic — and also the most difficult to master.

---

## What It Achieves

Given a little-finger break under a specific card, the pass brings that card immediately to the top of the deck in one action. To the audience, the hands simply square the deck. One moment the deck is in one order; the next moment, invisibly, it is in another.

The pass can also be used in the reverse direction to apparently "lose" the top card in the middle — and it can be used as a complete deck-restoration tool after a legitimate cut.

---

## Why It's Considered the Pinnacle

No other control offers the same speed and invisibility. A well-executed pass creates a moment of pure magic: the selected card has no possible location except in the middle of the deck, yet the performer immediately names or produces it from the top. No shuffle, no cut, no additional handling — just the snap of two hands coming together.

Giobbi writes that the pass is often treated with mystique by students of card magic, and that mystique is deserved — but it can also mislead students into thinking mastery requires supernatural speed. It does not. It requires **smoothness** and **cover**, not speed.

---

## The Classic Pass (CC2 Ch. 19)

The standard technique, taught in two stages:

**Stage 1 — The Mechanics:**

The deck is in left-hand dealing position. A little-finger break is held under the selected card (the selected card is above the break; everything else is below).

1. The right hand approaches from above as if to square the deck
2. The right thumb and fingers grip the portion *below* the break (the lower half)
3. Simultaneously: the right hand's fingers pull the lower half outward and up; the left hand's fingers curl upward to allow the upper half to swivel down into the right hand's grip
4. The two halves exchange positions — the lower half goes on top, the upper half goes below
5. The left hand closes around the reassembled deck

**Stage 2 — The Cover:**

The mechanics of the Classic Pass produce a slight ripple of motion visible from certain angles. Every form of teaching the pass focuses heavily on **cover** — an action that occupies the audience's attention during the fraction of a second the exchange occurs.

Giobbi teaches several covers:
- **The riffle pass** — a simultaneous riffle at the corner of the deck provides sound and visual motion that masks the exchange
- **Other covers** — turning slightly, reaching toward a spectator, or any natural body motion that shifts attention

---

## The Riffle Pass (CC2 Ch. 19)

The most practical form of the pass for most performers. The riffle provides two functions:

1. **Sound:** The brief riffling noise gives the audience something to hear during the action, masking the subtle sounds of the halves exchanging
2. **Visual motion:** The ripple of the riffle at one corner draws the eye away from the main exchange motion

In practice, many performers use the Classic Pass motion but integrate the riffle into every execution as a matter of habit — making the riffle pass effectively their default pass.

## The Advanced Pass Techniques (CC4 Ch. 49)

Giobbi returns to the pass in CC4 with more sophisticated versions:

### The Basic Technique (Revisited)
A refinement of the CC2 technique with added attention to the angle of the hands and the role of the right thumb in preventing the deck from "spreading" during the exchange.

### The Invisible Pass
The most deceptive form. The pass is timed to coincide with an action that provides maximum cover — typically the moment the performer turns to face a different spectator, spreads the deck slightly, or picks up the deck from the table. The "invisibility" comes from the cover, not the speed.

### Cover Suggestions for the Invisible Pass
A complete catalog of actions that can provide cover: dribbling, squaring, turning, waving, and several choreographic sequences.

### The Turnover Pass
The pass is executed simultaneously with turning the top card face up. The top card is turned as cover; beneath it the pass occurs. The spectator's eye follows the turned card and never registers the exchange.

### The Spread Pass
The deck is briefly spread, then the spread is closed — and the pass occurs during the closing action.

### The Dribble Pass
The pass is combined with a dribble from one hand to the other.

### The Cover Pass
A pass where the right hand covers the deck completely during the exchange, providing total visual concealment.

### The Display Pass
The pass is hidden within a display or flourish.

### The Christ Twist
A specific pass technique that involves a twist of the wrist, named after Henry Christ. A different mechanical approach that some performers find more natural.

---

## The Half-pass (CC4 Ch. 49)

**The Spread Half-pass:** Rather than transposing the entire deck, only one portion is reversed — used to reverse a card (or cards) in the deck secretly. The half-pass combined with a spread looks like the performer is simply showing cards in a spread.

## The Ongoing Debate: Should You Learn the Pass?

There has been debate throughout card magic history about whether the pass is worth learning given its difficulty:

**Arguments for:**
- No other sleight achieves the same instantaneous, clean control
- It is the most direct way to bring a card to the top without any shuffle or cut whatsoever
- For certain effects, only the pass creates the right conditions

**Arguments against:**
- For most performance situations, the Convincing Control or the Bluff Pass achieves a practically identical result with far less difficulty
- The pass's failure mode — a visible, sudden motion — is catastrophic; other controls' failure modes are more recoverable
- Many great card performers never use the pass in performance

**Giobbi's position:** He teaches the pass thoroughly and advocates for learning it, but repeatedly acknowledges that it is the performer's most demanding technical challenge and that excellent card magic can be performed without it. He does not treat it as a rite of passage to be executed carelessly, but as a technique to be mastered fully or not used at all.
