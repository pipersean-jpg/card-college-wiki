# False Dealing

**Sources:** CC4 Ch. 46 (pp. 929–944)

---

## What False Dealing Is

**False dealing** refers to the sleight-of-hand techniques used by card cheats to deal from positions other than the top of the deck while appearing to deal normally from the top. The two primary techniques are:

- **The Second Deal:** Dealing the second card while retaining the top card
- **The Bottom Deal:** Dealing from the bottom of the deck while appearing to deal from the top

These techniques are the province of the card cheat — they are used in gambling to deal good cards to accomplices or the cheat themselves while giving spectators or opponents apparently random cards. In magic, they are used in gambling demonstrations and in specific tricks where dealing from a controlled position is needed.

---

## The Cards and The Grip (CC4 Ch. 46)

Giobbi opens this chapter with an observation about the physical demands: false dealing is among the most technically challenging sleights, requiring extremely sensitive finger control and extensive practice. The style of grip used dramatically affects which deals are possible.

**The Basic Grip:** A variation of the dealing position where the deck is held slightly more firmly, with specific finger contacts that allow the top card to be retained while the second card (or bottom card) is freed.

**The True Deal:** Before learning false deals, Giobbi establishes what a genuine deal looks like — again, the principle of establishing baseline handling before the false action.

---

## The Second Deal

The second deal retains the top card (a known card, often a force card or a set card) while dealing the second card from the top. The audience believes the top card is being dealt.

### The Push-off Second Deal

The left thumb pushes the top card to the right (as in a normal deal). But instead of the right hand taking the top card, the right thumb contacts the second card (now exposed below the pushed-off top card) and deals it. The left thumb pulls the top card back to its position.

The push-off is the more visible version — the top card is visibly pushed right — and requires that this push be identical to the genuine deal.

### The Strike Second Deal

The top card is not pushed off. Instead, the right hand strikes at the deck from above, and the right thumb contacts the second card from the front rather than taking the top card. The top card remains in place.

The strike version is considered more deceptive because the top card never moves, but it is more difficult to execute cleanly.

---

## The Bottom Deal

The bottom card is dealt instead of the top card. The left hand's fingers peel the bottom card outward while the left thumb pushes the top card to the right as a cover (making it look like a top deal); the right hand takes the bottom card.

The bottom deal is used in demonstrations of "bottom dealing" for card cheats — dealing yourself the known card from the bottom.

### The Sail

A variation of the bottom deal where the bottom card is "sailed" out from the deck in a more extended, graceful motion. Used in some theatrical demonstrations.

---

## Practical Application in Magic

False dealing is rarely used in casual close-up magic — the techniques are demanding and the practical applications are limited. They are most used in:

- **Gambling demonstrations:** The performer demonstrates how card cheats stack and deal, showing the audience exactly how they would be cheated
- **Specific set-up tricks:** Where a pre-arranged deck must be dealt in a controlled order

The trick **Call Your Hand** (CC4) builds a complete poker-demonstration routine around false dealing.

## The Historical Context: Erdnase

The techniques of second and bottom dealing are documented extensively in **S.W. Erdnase's** *The Expert at the Card Table* (1902), which remains the primary historical source. Giobbi's treatment builds on Erdnase but incorporates 20th-century refinements. The strike second deal in particular was refined by numerous 20th-century card workers. See [S.W. Erdnase](originators/sw-erdnase.md).
