# The Glimpse

**Sources:** CC2 Ch. 23 (pp. 353–363)

---

## What the Glimpse Is

A **glimpse** is a secret look at the face of a card — learning its identity without the audience knowing. Once the performer knows a card's identity, that card becomes a key card or a force reference point.

Unlike the key card (where the performer places a known card next to the selection), the glimpse is done *after* the selection: the performer sees the selection (or a specific card) and uses that knowledge to perform the location or revelation.

---

## Top-card Riffle Glimpse

The most useful and natural-looking glimpse method. As the performer rifles the corner of the deck, the top card is held slightly apart — and the performer's downward angle reveals the face of the top card during the riffle action. From the front, the performer appears to be simply riffling the cards.

Used constantly in practice: any time the deck is riffled (for a force, for a spread, for any reason), the top card can be glimpsed without any additional handling.

---

## Top-card Double Lift Glimpse

During the action of a double lift — as the two cards are turned over — the second card's face is briefly visible to the performer at the inner end. This glimpse reveals what card is second from the top.

This makes the double lift doubly useful: it both shows a false card to the audience and simultaneously reveals the hidden card to the performer.

---

## Top-card Riffle Shuffle Glimpse

During a genuine riffle shuffle, the top card can be seen at the end of the shuffle as packets are squared. A specific handling angle allows this without any additional preparation.

---

## Bottom Card — All-around Square-up Glimpse

During the all-around square-up action (squaring the deck from all four sides), the deck is momentarily tilted enough that the bottom card's face is seen by the performer.

This is one of the most natural and brief glimpse moments: the squaring action is completely expected and unremarkable; the glimpse is a byproduct of a normal handling.

---

## Situational Bottom Card Glimpses

Giobbi describes several situation-dependent glimpses: moments during ordinary handling — placing the deck on the table, picking it up, handing it to a spectator — where the bottom card's face naturally comes into the performer's view. These require no technique; they require awareness of when to look.

---

## Center-card Glimpse

Seeing a card in the middle of the deck. This is more complex than a top or bottom glimpse. Methods include:
- Spreading the deck and using the angle of the spread
- Using a peek (having the spectator look at a card in the middle while the performer glimpses it from the back)

---

## The Glimpse as a Habitual Skill

Giobbi makes a strong philosophical point: the glimpse is not just a technique — it is a **perceptual habit**. Experienced card workers are always gathering information passively during normal handling. They glimpse the top card when riffling, the bottom card when squaring, and cards at specific positions during dealing — not because they are executing a deliberate technique, but because they have trained themselves to notice.

This constant awareness is one of the key differences between novice and expert card workers. The expert always knows more about the current state of the deck than they appear to.
