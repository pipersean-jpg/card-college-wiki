# False Cuts

**Sources:** CC1 Ch. 3 (pp. 55–62); CC1 Ch. 6 (pp. 93–101); CC2 Ch. 25 (pp. 379–392)

---

## What a False Cut Is

A **false cut** looks like a genuine cut of the deck — one or more packets are moved and restacked — but leaves the deck in its original order (or a specific predetermined order). False cuts are used to:

1. **Reinforce a shuffle** — after a false shuffle, a false cut makes the deck appear even more thoroughly mixed
2. **Maintain a setup** — when the audience expects you to cut the deck, do so without disturbing the order
3. **Restore the deck** — after genuinely cutting for a spectator, a false cut can return the deck to its original condition

False cuts complement false shuffles. They are more visual and feel more decisive — a cut is a "fair" action in most people's minds because it appears to move the cards decisively.

---

## Transfer Cuts (CC1 Ch. 6)

Giobbi classifies these separately as "transfer cuts" — cuts that genuinely *do* transfer the position of the top card. They move the deck from one arrangement to another in a controlled way rather than simply preserving the order.

**The Double Cut:** The right hand takes roughly the bottom two-thirds of the deck, then the top third is placed on the bottom. The result: the deck looks cut, but a card controlled to the bottom is now on top. This is the most commonly used method for bringing a card controlled to the bottom up to the top.

**The Triple Cut from Top to Bottom / Bottom to Top:** Three packet cuts, each moving a portion, with the net result of moving the top stock to the bottom (or vice versa).

Transfer cuts are used constantly in conjunction with overhand shuffle controls — the card is controlled to the bottom, and a double cut brings it to the top.

## Optical False Cuts (CC1 Ch. 3)

### An Optical False Cut from the Hand

The deck is held in dealing position. The right hand lifts the top half and taps it against the bottom half's edge, then places it back. The motion looks like the beginning of a cut, but the deck returns to its original state. The "optical" element: the motion implies a cut occurred without actually executing one.

### The False Swing Cut

The deck is held in dealing position. The top half swings out on the left thumb (the motion of a genuine swing cut), but instead of placing it below, the right hand deposits it back on top in the squaring action. Very fast, and effective when performed casually during conversation.

### A Simple False Cut

A one-handed sequence where the deck appears to be cut but a small packet is kept in reserve and placed back in its original position.

### A Triple False Cut

Three packets are moved in sequence, producing the visual impression of a thorough three-way cut, but the deck is restored to its original state.

### The Slip Cut

Performed from dealing position: the right hand takes the top half, and as it moves away, the left thumb "slips" one card from the top of the right hand's packet back onto the left hand's packet. This moves the top card to a different position while appearing to be an innocent cut.

## Advanced False Cuts (CC2 Ch. 25)

### A True, Single, Tabled Cut

Before teaching false tabled cuts, Giobbi establishes what a genuine tabled cut looks like — the same attention to establishing a baseline as with the overhand shuffle.

### Single Tabled False Cut I and II

Two methods for performing a complete false cut at the table. Both move packets in ways that appear decisive but return the deck to its original order.

### Up the Ladder

A sequence of multiple cuts performed in rapid succession — cutting from the bottom, then the top, alternating — that produces a completely fair-looking thorough cutting yet returns the deck to its starting order.

### Dai Vernon's Cold-deck Cut

**Attributed to:** Dai Vernon

A specific tabled false cut sequence developed by Vernon for use in gambling demonstrations. It appears to be multiple fair cuts but preserves the deck order throughout. This is one of the more difficult false cuts in the series and is used specifically in contexts where the performer is demonstrating cheating techniques.

### The Affas-gaffas False Cut

A compound sequence of multiple cuts using both hands simultaneously, each cut covering the next. Visually complex and highly deceptive.

### The Gambler's False Triple Cut

A gambling-themed three-packet false cut performed with the nonchalance of a card cheat.

## Jack Merlin's Riffle Shuffle (CC1 Ch. 12 / Flourishes)

**Attributed to:** Jack Merlin

Giobbi includes this in the flourishes section of CC1, but it functions as a false shuffle: a spring-and-riffle action that appears to mix the cards but actually preserves the top stock. It has the quality of a display move that doubles as a false shuffle — practical and showy simultaneously.

## What the Sources Agree On

False cuts are almost always more convincing when:
1. They are performed as an **afterthought** — casually, during conversation, not as a deliberate "look, I'm cutting" action
2. They are **followed by something else immediately** — the audience's attention moves to the next phase
3. They are **consistent with the setting** — a tabled cut in a standing performance looks awkward

---

## Debate: Are False Cuts Necessary?

Some card workers argue that if a false shuffle is good enough, a false cut adds little. Others argue that the combination of false shuffle + false cut creates a layered defense that is much harder for skeptics to penetrate mentally.

Giobbi takes a pragmatic position: use false cuts when the performance context calls for a "cutting" action from the audience's perspective, or when a spectator wants to cut the deck themselves (in which case a double cut or transfer cut can restore the order after a genuine cut).
