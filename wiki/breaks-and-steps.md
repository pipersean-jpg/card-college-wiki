# Breaks and Steps

**Sources:** CC1 Ch. 1 (pp. 28–34); CC3 Ch. 29 (pp. 527–543)

---

## What This Is

A **break** is a tiny, invisible gap secretly held open between two portions of the deck. A **step** is an off-square condition of the deck where a portion protrudes slightly. Both serve the same purpose: **marking a position** — usually a spectator's returned card, or the top of a specific packet — so that a cut or sleight can later act on that exact location.

Breaks and steps are the hidden scaffolding of card magic. Dozens of controls, forces, passes, and multiple lifts depend on first obtaining one.

---

## The Little-Finger Break

The most important break in card magic. The left little finger holds a separation between two portions of the deck — so small from the outside that it is invisible, yet large enough for the performer to feel it precisely.

**How it works:**  
While the deck is held in left-hand dealing position, the left little finger presses inward against the right edge at the break point, separating upper and lower portions by roughly the thickness of a thumbnail. From the front, the deck appears perfectly squared.

**Two-handed method:** The right hand squares the deck from above while simultaneously inserting the left little finger into the desired position. The right hand's covering motion conceals the pinch.

**One-handed method:** The left thumb pushes the top card(s) sideways to produce a gap, which the little finger then enters.

**Multiple cards:** By inserting the little finger under several cards at once (using the little-finger count or thumb count), a break under any number of cards can be obtained.

Giobbi notes that the little-finger break is the universal get-ready for dozens of subsequent sleights including the pass, the double lift, multiple lifts, and palming.

---

## The Thumb Break

The right thumb holds a similar separation at the inner end of the deck. Whereas the little-finger break is maintained as the deck stays in the left hand, the thumb break is typically used when the right hand takes the deck by the ends (end grip).

A thumb break is easier to acquire than a little-finger break and is preferred for moves executed from the end grip, such as the riffle force and various palm handlings.

---

## The Step

A step is produced when a portion of the deck protrudes at one end or one side — pushed slightly out of square by a finger. Unlike a break (which is a gap *inside* the deck), the step is visible as a physical protrusion at the edge, but because it is small and the performer's handling is smooth, spectators do not register it.

The step is used mainly at tables where one hand is unavailable, or when the performer needs a longer-duration locator without maintaining muscular tension (as a held break requires). A spectator can even be asked to hold a stepped deck, as Giobbi demonstrates in CC3.

**Ribbon spread and step:** When cards are spread across the table, a step can be formed by pushing one packet sideways before squaring. This allows picking up the deck with the step intact.

**Fan and step:** A similar technique applies when cards are fanned.

---

## The Riffle

Not the shuffle — a specific action at the corner of the deck. The left thumb releases cards from the corner of the deck one at a time, producing the "riffle" sound and tactile sensation. This is used:

- To cut the deck to a specific location (for a spectator to insert a card)
- As a cover for producing a break
- In the riffle force, riffle glimpse, and riffle shuffle

The riffle is taught in CC1 as a fundamental tool rather than a sleight. Mastering consistent finger pressure on the riffle is prerequisite to the riffle force and many glimpse methods.

## Advanced Break Work (CC3 Ch. 29)

Volume 3 expands break technique considerably:

- **The Dribble Break:** A break obtained as cards are dribbled from one hand to the other — useful because the motion of dribbling provides natural cover
- **The Crocodile Technique:** Clamping and releasing the deck with the left hand in a way that appears like simple squaring while actually setting a break
- **Transferring a break** from the left little finger to the heel of the right thumb — essential for moves that require changing the holding hand
- **Inserting a card into a break:** Making a returned card look casually replaced while actually placing it above or below the break
- **The Erdnase Break:** A specific break position noted in *The Expert at the Card Table*, used in Erdnase's sequences

---

## Injogged Cards (CC3 Ch. 29)

An **injog** is a card pushed inward (toward the performer) so that it sticks out at the inner end while the rest of the deck is held in dealing position. It serves as a visible-to-the-performer marker, similar to a step.

- **LePaul Automatic Jog-Control:** Named for magician Paul LePaul, this method controls an injogged card to the top with a single clean motion during an overhand shuffle run-up
- **Drop Jog:** Allowing a card to fall injogged during a spread
- **Diagonal Insertion:** Inserting a card diagonally so that only its corner jogs out

Giobbi reflects that injogs and steps are sometimes superior to breaks because they require no muscular tension and can be maintained for longer periods without discomfort.
