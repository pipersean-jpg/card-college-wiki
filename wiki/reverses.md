# Reverses

**Sources:** CC2 Ch. 24 (pp. 365–376); CC4 Ch. 52 (pp. 1027–1041)

---

## What a Reverse Is

A **reverse** secretly turns one or more cards face-up within a face-down deck (or face-down within a face-up deck). When the deck is later spread, the reversed card appears to have "traveled" there — or its face-up orientation serves as the climax of a trick.

Reverses are the mechanism behind many "signed card appears face-up" effects, discovery sequences, and themed routines like Triumph.

---

## Volume 2, Chapter 24

### The Braue Reverse

**Attributed to:** Frederick Braue

The most important basic reverse. The deck is held in dealing position. A small packet at the top (or a single card) is pivoted downward behind the deck and then the deck is turned over as a whole — or, the top card is pushed over and under the deck face-up.

In the standard Braue Reverse: the selected card is on top. The performer's right hand lifts a portion of the deck; the left hand turns its remaining cards face-up; the packets are reassembled. When the deck is next spread, the selected card appears face-up among face-down cards.

Frederick Braue, who collaborated with Jean Hugard on *The Royal Road to Card Magic*, is credited with this elegant, clean reverse technique.

### Double Lift Reverse

The top two cards are taken as one (double lift) and flipped over — but instead of being replaced on top, they are slipped underneath the deck while still turned over. The result: one card is reversed in the deck.

### Double Turnover Reverse

A reverse integrated with a double turnover — as the double is turned face-up, the bottom card of the double is kept face-up when the pair is returned.

### The Leg Reverse

A reverse executed below the table — the card passes below the table edge momentarily, during which it is reversed. Used at a table where the under-the-table space provides cover.

### Deck Turnover Reverse

The entire deck is turned over as cover for reversing a specific card or packet.

## Volume 4, Chapter 52 — Advanced Reverses

### The Swing-cut Reverse

A reverse integrated with a swing cut — the card is reversed during the natural motion of a cut.

### The Future Reverse

A reverse that is set up in advance for a later reveal — the reversed card is placed in the deck before the trick officially begins, to be discovered only at the climax.

### The Palm Reverse

A card is palmed face-up (or face-down when the deck is face-up), then added back to the deck during any natural palm replacement — arriving reversed.

### The Larreverse

**Name:** A portmanteau — "Larr" + "reverse." Giobbi uses this to indicate the move's originator or describing quality.

A specific reverse using a distinctive wrist motion.

### FIRP

**"FIRP"** stands for **Face-up Insertion Reverse Placement** — a compound description of the action. A specific sequence for inserting a face-up card into a face-down deck (or vice versa) without any visible turning action.

### The Riffle-shuffle Reverse

A reverse integrated into a riffle shuffle — the card is introduced face-up during the shuffle's interleave.

## The Triumph Connection

The Triumph Shuffle (CC3 Ch. 33) is specifically designed around reversal: half the deck is turned face-up and shuffled with the face-down half, then apparently "magically" restored. The reverse techniques here provide the tools to execute Triumph variations and related effects.

---

## Attribution Notes

The **Braue Reverse** is one of the more solidly attributed techniques in Giobbi's series. Frederick Braue and Jean Hugard co-authored *The Royal Road to Card Magic*, one of the most important English-language card magic books, and Braue's contributions to technique are well-documented.
