    The Faro Shuffle¶

    Sources: CC3 Ch. 35 (pp. 681–701)

    What This Is¶

    The Faro Shuffle (also called the Perfect Shuffle or Weave Shuffle) is a riffle shuffle in which the two halves of the deck interleave perfectly — every card from one half sits between two cards from the other half, alternating with mathematical precision. Unlike the Zarrow Shuffle or Pull-Through (which are false shuffles), the Faro genuinely mixes the cards — but in a completely predictable, reversible pattern.

    This predictability is what makes the Faro one of the most powerful tools in structured card magic: the performer can know exactly where every card will be after a specific number of Faro shuffles.

    Types of Faro Shuffle¶

    The In-Shuffle¶

    The two halves interleave such that the first card of the right packet goes between the top card and second card of the left packet. The result: the original top card is now in position 2. This "pushes" the deck inward.

    The Out-Shuffle¶

    The two halves interleave such that the original top card remains on top and the original bottom card remains on the bottom. Everything in between shifts one position toward the center.

    The distinction is critical: eight perfect out-shuffles return a 52-card deck to its original order. Eight perfect in-shuffles do the same. Mixing in-shuffles and out-shuffles produces different mathematical outcomes.

    The Partial Faro (CC3 Ch. 35)¶

    A Faro shuffle performed with only part of the deck — one complete half and a partial packet. Used to place a known card at a specific position within a larger portion of the deck.

    The Faro Slough-off (CC3 Ch. 35)¶

    A technique for completing the Faro by pushing the two interlocked halves together in a "slough-off" action. This is not a mechanical action but a handling refinement for squaring the deck cleanly after the interleave.

    The Incomplete Faro Control (CC3 Ch. 35)¶

    Using a partially-completed Faro as a control — the interleave stops at a specific point, allowing the performer to know exactly where a particular card has arrived.

    The Mathematical Basis (CC3 Ch. 35)¶

    Giobbi includes a detailed treatment of the mathematics. Key facts:

    • Eight out-shuffles return the deck to its original order
    • After n out-shuffles, the card originally at position k moves to position k × 2^n (mod 51)
    • The Faro can be used to stack any card to any position in a known number of shuffles
    • 26 in-shuffles return the deck to its original order (different number due to the position shift)

    This mathematics is the foundation of many "mathematical" card tricks where cards appear at impossible positions after a series of shuffles.

    Performing the Perfect Faro¶

    Executing a perfect Faro requires: 1. The deck must be split into exactly 26 cards in each half — not approximately, but precisely 2. The corners of the two halves must align perfectly before the weave 3. The right-hand packet must push through the left-hand packet with even pressure so every card interleaves with its neighbor

    In practice, the Faro is one of the most technically demanding techniques in card magic. Even minor variations in card condition (soft, old, or slightly damaged cards) make a perfect Faro impossible.

    Giobbi recommends that new cards, broken in only slightly, are essential. He also notes that the feel of the weave is specific — it should feel like a zipper closing, not a push through resistance.

    Applications in Tricks¶

    Three complete tricks built on Faro mathematics appear in CC3:

    • The Acrobatic Aces: A multi-phase ace routine using Faro positioning
    • The Two Detectives: A story trick using Faro principles for placement
    • Numerology: A counting effect derived entirely from Faro mathematics

    Debate: Practicality of the Faro¶

    Giobbi is unusually candid about the limitations: the Faro is not universally practical. It requires new cards in good condition, a quiet environment (the shuffle makes a distinctive sound), and a high level of technical skill. Many professional performers prefer stacking methods or mathematical set-ups that do not require perfect Faro execution.

    The counterargument: once mastered, the Faro creates effects of seemingly impossible mathematical precision that no other method can match.
