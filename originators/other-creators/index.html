    Other Creators¶

    This article collects brief profiles of all other named originators that Giobbi credits throughout the Card College series.

    Jean-Eugène Robert-Houdin (1805–1871)¶

    Nationality: French
    Contribution in Giobbi: The improved handling of the Top Change (CC1 Ch. 16). Giobbi attributes the specific top-change handling to Robert-Houdin and quotes him in the chapter epigraph: "The more simple and natural the movements of the performer, the less likely is the spectator to detect the trick."

    Robert-Houdin is the most important figure in the history of stage magic. A clockmaker by training, he transformed magic performance from vulgar street spectacle into elegant theater and is often called "the father of modern magic." His memoirs were the inspiration for Harry Houdini's stage name.

    Frederick Braue (1908–1963)¶

    Nationality: American
    Contributions in Giobbi:

    • The Braue Reverse (CC2 Ch. 24) — the foundational one-card reverse technique
    • The Braue Addition (CC1 Ch. 14) — a technique for secretly adding cards to a displayed packet

    Braue co-authored The Royal Road to Card Magic (1948) with Jean Hugard — the book that served as the standard English-language card magic course before Card College. Juan Tamariz mentions this book in the CC1 foreword as Giobbi's predecessor.

    Alex Elmsley (1929–2006)¶

    Nationality: British
    Contribution in Giobbi: The Elmsley Count (CC2 Ch. 20) — the most widely used false count in close-up card magic. A mathematician who applied rigorous analytical thinking to card magic.

    Brother John Hamman (1916–1993)¶

    Nationality: American (Catholic brother)
    Contributions in Giobbi: - The Hamman Count (CC2 Ch. 20) - The Hamman Two-for-four Count (CC2 Ch. 20)

    Herb Zarrow (1920–2009)¶

    Nationality: American
    Contribution in Giobbi: The Zarrow Shuffle (CC3 Ch. 33) — the tabled riffle false shuffle that retains the complete deck order. See The Zarrow Shuffle.

    Harry Houdini (1874–1926)¶

    Real name: Erik Weisz
    Nationality: American (Hungarian-born)
    Contribution in Giobbi: The Houdini Color Change (CC3 Ch. 37) — a color change in which the hand wipes across the face of the deck, leaving a different card revealed. See Color Changes.

    Houdini's name is familiar to all audiences, which can be used to theatrical advantage when performing the Houdini Color Change with appropriate context.

    Jack Merlin (fl. early 20th century)¶

    Nationality: American
    Contribution in Giobbi: Jack Merlin's Riffle Shuffle (CC1 Ch. 12) — a one-handed spring-and-riffle shuffle performed as a flourish. It serves a dual purpose as both a visual display and a practical (if unusual) false shuffle.

    Cardini (1895–1973)¶

    Real name: Richard Valentine Pitchford
    Nationality: Welsh-born, American
    Contribution in Giobbi: Cardini's Overhand Multiple Shift (CC3 Ch. 34) — a multiple shift performed during an overhand shuffle. See The Multiple Shift.

    Cardini was famous for his silent manipulation act, performed while wearing monocle and white gloves.

    John Carney (born 1959)¶

    Nationality: American
    Contribution in Giobbi: Carney's Natural Multiple Shift (CC3 Ch. 34) — a version of the multiple shift emphasizing extreme naturalness of handling.

    Paul LePaul (1900–1958)¶

    Nationality: American
    Contribution in Giobbi: The LePaul Automatic Jog-control (CC3 Ch. 29) — a method for controlling an injogged card to the top during an overhand shuffle sequence. Named for Paul LePaul, author of The Card Magic of LePaul.

    Slydini (1901–1991)¶

    Real name: Quintino Marucci (Tony Slydini)
    Nationality: Italian-born, American
    Contribution in Giobbi: The Strong/Weak Moments theory (CC2 Ch. 27) — the concept that every performance has moments of high and low audience attention, and that sleights should be executed during low-attention (weak) moments. Giobbi also cites Slydini's performance advice: "A good general chooses his battleground."

    Slydini was known for lapwork and for seemingly impossible close-up magic achieved without apparent technique.

    Fred Kaps (1926–1980)¶

    Nationality: Dutch
    Contribution in Giobbi: The Kaps Switch (CC4 Ch. 40) — a card switch technique. See Card Switches.

    Fred Kaps was winner of the FISM Grand Prix (world magic championship) multiple times and is considered one of the finest close-up performers of the twentieth century.

    Ed Balducci¶

    Contribution in Giobbi: The Balducci Force / Crisscross Force (CC1 Ch. 5). See Other Forces.

    Goldin¶

    Contribution in Giobbi: The Goldin Force (CC1 Ch. 5) — a spread-based force method.

    Charles T. Jordan (1888–1944)¶

    Nationality: American
    Contribution in Giobbi: The Jordan Count (CC2 Ch. 20) — a false count that adds a card to a packet's apparent number.

    Norman Houghton¶

    Contribution in Giobbi: Norman Houghton's Pocket Extraction (CC3 Ch. 36) — a sequence for producing a palmed card from the jacket pocket. See Bottom Palm and Gambler's Cop.

    John Veeser¶

    Contribution in Giobbi: The Veeser Concept (CC4 Ch. 41) — the foundational principle underlying packet switches. See Packet Switches.

    Jean Hugard (1872–1959)¶

    Nationality: Australian, later American
    Note: Not credited for a specific technique in the Giobbi series, but Hugard's co-authored The Royal Road to Card Magic (with Frederick Braue) is referenced in the CC1 foreword by Tamariz as the previous generation's standard card magic course. Giobbi positions Card College as the successor to that tradition.

    Henry Christ¶

    Contribution in Giobbi: The Christ Twist (CC4 Ch. 49) — a specific pass variant using a distinctive wrist twist. See The Pass.

    Jean-Claude Pages¶

    Contribution in Giobbi: Jean-Claude Pages's Lapping Method (CC4 Ch. 48) — a natural-looking lapping technique by the French close-up worker. See Lapping.

    Paul Curry (1917–1986)¶

    Nationality: American
    Contribution in Giobbi: The Curry Turnover Change (CC4 Ch. 40) — a card switch where a face-down card is turned over using another card as the lever, and the two exchange secretly. See Card Switches.

    Curry is best known as the creator of the "Out of This World" effect, considered by many magicians to be one of the greatest card tricks ever devised.

    Nate Leipzig (1873–1939)¶

    Nationality: American (Swedish-born)
    Contribution in Giobbi: Not a technique attribution, but a performance philosophy: Giobbi cites Leipzig's advice in CC2 Ch. 27: "Don't ever perform unless coaxed." Leipzig was one of the first performers to develop a reputation for intimate, conversational close-up magic.
